INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — ANTECHAMBER CORRIDOR — CONTINUOUS

She is three steps from the outer door when the secondary door opens.

Roman does not appear. He was not hiding. The door opens and he is already moving — not erupting from the room, not charging: moving with the specific economy of a person who decided several seconds ago what the next action was going to be and is now executing it. He heard the shutdown sequence initiate. He has had forty-three seconds.

He knows where the walls are. He knows the corridor width, the ceiling height, the door frame clearance, the one uneven seam in the floor six feet from the server core entry. He has walked this space for eleven years.

She has been in it for eight minutes.

The first exchange is fast and incomplete. She reads his line and adjusts — not fully, the vasovagal debt catches up in the pivot, she is a quarter-second behind where the drill says to be. The deflection is partial. His forearm catches her jaw instead of her temple. It is not the intended hit. It is still hard.

She takes it.

She gives back a palm strike to the sternum — controlled, targeted at the xiphoid, calibrated to stun rather than damage the cardiac. He takes it in the chest wall instead, his rotation off. The stun does not land. The force does.

Both of them reset.

He is faster in this space than she expected. She made an estimate of him based on the broadcast footage, the documented history of the events, the physical profile in Morrow's files. The estimate was accurate. It was not complete. What the files did not have: the way he moves when no one is watching, when there is no audience for it, when the only function of the movement is to end this.

He is better without the camera than with it.

He catches her on the second approach — a controlled grab, corridor geometry used precisely, her trajectory constrained by the wall behind her before she can establish a counter-line. His grip on her left arm is mechanical, practiced, the kind of hold that is maintained by leverage not strength. She needs two moves to break it. She gets one.

He uses the partial break. Her left side opens. He drives his elbow into her ribs — not a swing, a deliberate placement, weight transferred through the joint: a close-range impact that doesn't read as dramatic and lands at maximum yield.

She hears the creak.

Not break. Creak. Two, possibly three ribs under acute compression. She has three seconds before the pain response arrives — trained for this, she uses the three seconds. She drives her knee into his thigh at the femoral nerve cluster, misses center, catches the margin. He staggers.

The pain arrives.

She breathes in. The rib cost is immediate and specific: the left side of her thorax compresses on the inhale, the capacity on that side is reduced by a fraction she can measure. She has had worse. She has not had this at the end of what she has already done today.

She is still standing. She is still functional. The margin is narrower than it was.

Across the corridor, Roman is straightening.

He is not done. He is breathing carefully — the sternum impact has left something. Not the intended effect. Enough.

He looks at her the way she has not seen him look at anything: without the register in place, without the calibration. Just assessment. Just this room and what is in it.

She looks back.

The building is quiet. The machine is gone and the building is quiet and there is nothing between them except the corridor and the decision she made in the trophy room and whatever it costs to keep it.

She adjusts her weight off the left side.
