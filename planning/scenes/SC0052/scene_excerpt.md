INT. EASTBOUND TRAIN — FORWARD-FACING SEAT — LATE MORNING

She is back in the broadcast architecture section. The relay installation diagrams. She has been here for twelve minutes since she closed the event history.

Then she goes back.

The collection section is still open in a secondary tab — she left it half-read after the archive description caught her. She opens it fully now.

Marcus documented the trophy room in two distinct passes. The first pass is the physical description: location, security classification, access restrictions. She read this in the event history section. The second pass is what she did not read yet: his analysis of the archive's contents.

Marcus categorized the items by type. Physical trophies — correct, one per event, organized chronologically. But the second category is what Marcus flagged: operational documents. Entry agreements from each event. Payment records. And for the broadcast events — a full run of four — broadcast access layer documentation.

She reads this twice.

Roman keeps the broadcast access layer credentials inside the archive. Not in the broadcast infrastructure's network layer. Not offsite in a separate secure facility. Physical. Inside the collection. Marcus wrote this as a finding, not a speculation: sourced from two independent channels, verified against the archive's known access log.

She goes to the next section. Marcus's analysis of the pattern.

The pattern Marcus identified: Roman does not trust digital infrastructure alone for mechanisms he considers irreplaceable. He maintains physical analogs for the keys that matter most. The broadcast access layer credentials are in the archive because, to Roman, the broadcast is the machine — and the machine's key belongs with his trophies. The broadcast is how he wins. The key to the broadcast stays where his wins are kept.

She holds this for a moment.

Then she reads Marcus's working hypothesis. He wrote it as a hypothesis, clearly labeled: the shutdown code for the broadcast architecture — the mechanism identified in the relay installation documentation — almost certainly has a physical analog. Roman's archiving pattern strongly suggests it. If the shutdown code exists in any form that Roman would classify as a key to the machine, it is in the archive.

Marcus did not verify this. He flagged it as unverified. He wrote: *this is the inference the documentation supports; I have not confirmed it; it should be treated as a high-confidence hypothesis rather than a sourced finding.*

She reads the distinction he drew between sourced finding and high-confidence hypothesis. She considers it.

Then she sets it aside. Not because the distinction doesn't matter — it does — but because the alternative is that the shutdown code has no physical analog, which means it exists only inside the broadcast network infrastructure, which means there is no single physical object she can locate and use. The hypothesis is not confirmed. The hypothesis is also the only path that leads to a deployable mechanism. She proceeds on the hypothesis.

The trophy room is the target.

Not one of the relay installations. Not UNIT 7-ECHO, which she is still traveling toward, which still matters, which is still the preliminary access point. But the primary target — the object that ends the machine — is inside Roman's archive inside his private residence at VELTAIN.

She opens a new note on the device. She writes three things:

1. Trophy room — VELTAIN, private residence. Primary objective.
2. Shutdown code — physical analog, archive. Unverified/high-confidence.
3. Relay installations — preliminary. Access layer.

She looks at the list.

The third item is the one she has a path to. She is on a train toward it right now. Items one and two require her to get from the relay installation access layer to the inside of Roman's private residence. The documentation does not tell her how to do that. No section of Marcus's three hundred and forty pages addresses the path from the infrastructure perimeter to the residence interior.

She will need to find that path somewhere else, from someone who knows the facility, under conditions she cannot currently predict.

This is not a gap in the plan. It is the plan's open variable. It is the thing she doesn't have yet and cannot get until she's further inside the situation.

She closes the note. She goes back to the relay installation diagrams.

The train moves east. She keeps reading.
