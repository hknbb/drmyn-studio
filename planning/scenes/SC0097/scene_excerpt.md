INT. ORACLE PRIME BROADCAST OPERATIONS CENTER — NIGHT

The operations center is a long room — server infrastructure along the west wall, monitoring stations in a row facing the primary broadcast display. The primary display runs The Herald's feed on a three-meter screen. It is always on. It has been always on for four years.

MORROW is at the central station. Forty-seven years old, systems maintenance, the person in this building who knows what every cable does. He has been at this terminal for eleven hours. He will be here until someone tells him he can leave, which will not be tonight.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
(broadcast smooth)
— and the current position of our subject places her approximately seven kilometers from the primary operational zone, which our northern grid confirms with high confidence —

The feed stutters.

Not a cut — a stutter. Half a second where the audio degrades at a frequency that is not quite silence, and the visual feed drops one frame in seven, and anyone watching closely enough will see that The Herald's mouth — the HALO UNIT's constructed face — moves slightly out of sync with the sound.

The betting interface, running in the lower third of the primary display, flickers. The numbers hold. The Herald's honesty protocol is intact — the betting data is accurate — but the interface that surrounds it is visibly unstable.

Morrow is already looking at his diagnostics panel. He has been looking at it for forty seconds, which means he saw this coming and could not prevent it.

The interference is entering from three points simultaneously: the northern grid's relay node is saturated with a signal that his spectrum analyzer cannot identify as any standard broadcast source; the eastern approach corridor's proximity sensor network is reporting position data that conflicts with the primary grid's mapping; and the base HALO UNIT feed — the Herald's primary sensory infrastructure — is experiencing packet loss at a rate that should not be possible without physical disruption to the cable plant.

He has not seen three simultaneous points before. He has not seen one that he could not trace in forty seconds.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
(slight degradation — almost imperceptible)
— our subject's current trajectory suggests — our subject's current —

The feed stutters again. This time the audio cuts for three full seconds before the buffer compensates.

Then: silence.

The primary display is live but The Herald is not speaking. The HALO UNIT's face is present — the composed, pleasant construction that has been ORACLE PRIME's public interface for four years — and it is silent.

Seven seconds.

In the operations center, the silence sounds like something breaking.

Morrow pulls up the HALO UNIT maintenance interface. He has done this before — minor calibrations, feed quality checks, the routine maintenance that keeps the broadcast running at specification. He has never done it during a live broadcast. The protocol exists for scheduled maintenance windows, not for mid-broadcast intervention.

He opens the maintenance command set. Finds the override sequence: HALO-RESTORE / PRIMARY-FEED / BYPASS-ROUTING. It routes the broadcast directly through the HALO UNIT's base computational layer, bypassing the signal processing that runs through the compromised relay infrastructure.

He enters it.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
(returning — almost the same)
— we apologize for the brief interruption. Our systems are performing scheduled maintenance.

It is not scheduled maintenance. The Herald knows it is not scheduled maintenance. The Herald says what its current operational parameters tell it to say, and its current operational parameters — running now through the base layer rather than the full grid, with degraded spatial data from the interference-affected relay nodes — are producing language that hedges where it would normally assert.

THE HERALD (V.O.) (CONT'D)
It appears that our subject is currently positioned within the secondary residential district. We are confirming her exact location. Confidence is —

A pause. The Herald does not pause.

THE HERALD (V.O.) (CONT'D)
— confidence is high.

The declaration is correct. The pause before it is new. The audience — two hundred forty thousand active viewers at this hour — hears the pause. Some of them have been watching for months. None of them have heard The Herald pause before a confidence declaration.

The betting volume shifts. A small movement, not a panic. A fraction of the active positions update. The movement is in one direction: the odds on the subject surviving the current phase improve by three points in eleven seconds.

The audience is processing the pause.

Morrow watches the betting interface. He has seen the odds move in response to game events — that is the system functioning correctly. He has not seen them move in response to The Herald's voice quality.

His terminal chimes. An inbound connection request from the compound's internal communications network. He knows whose terminal that is.

He accepts the connection. Does not speak.

ROMAN (V.O.)
(over comms — measured, not raised)
What is the source.

MORROW
Three simultaneous. Northern relay saturation, eastern sensor conflict, base unit packet loss. I can't isolate any of them in real time. I've run the override to hold the primary feed.

ROMAN (V.O.)
Is the betting data intact.

MORROW
Yes. Honesty protocol is on the base layer — it's not affected by the routing.

A pause on Roman's end. The kind of pause that costs something.

ROMAN (V.O.)
How long until you can identify the source.

MORROW
The northern saturation signal — I don't recognize the encoding schema. If someone is feeding it deliberately, they've built it to look like nothing I have in the library. That takes time and access that shouldn't exist.

ROMAN (V.O.)
How long.

MORROW
Four hours to trace. Maybe less if the interference stops.

Another pause.

ROMAN (V.O.)
It won't stop.

The connection closes.

Morrow sits at his station. On the primary display, The Herald continues its broadcast — almost perfectly, the base layer holding well enough to maintain the surface. Only someone who knows the system as well as Morrow does would hear the difference. Only someone who has been watching very carefully for a very long time.

He pulls up the maintenance command log. The override entry is there, timestamped, fully documented: HALO-RESTORE / PRIMARY-FEED / BYPASS-ROUTING. Standard maintenance protocol. Emergency application.

He closes the log.
