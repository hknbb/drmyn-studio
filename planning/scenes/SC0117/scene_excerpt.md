The transit hub is directly ahead. Secondary hub, seventeen gates, one main concourse. She has not been inside it. She does not need to have been inside it. She knows where it opens on the other side.

She adjusts Jin to her right hip — the familiar geometry — and walks into the transit hub.

---

The concourse is occupied: commuters, a school group moving in pieces, a man reading at a bench, two people consulting a map at the center pillar, a vendor selling something from a cart that smells of sugar and oil. The noise is substantial. Jin presses his face into her shoulder — not distressed, calibrating; he is managing the input the way he manages new input, by reducing his visual field to the one surface he can control. She puts her hand against the back of his head. Not to reassure him. To reduce the surface further. He settles.

She walks through the concourse at the pace of people who are late for something ordinary. She is indistinguishable from them. A woman with a child in a transit hub in a city in the afternoon — there is nothing to see. The cover is the city at ordinary function and she is using it.

The ribs itemize every step. She does not stop counting the gates.

---

Twelve gates. The north exit. A set of doors that open onto a plaza she has not seen but which is, by her calculation, two blocks from the contact point.

She pushes through the doors.

---

The plaza. Open air. The contact point is at the southeast corner — a specific bench, a specific arrangement. She can see it from here. A person sitting at it, reading. Not looking up.

Not looking up yet.

She checks the time. The contact window was ten minutes. She has three remaining.

She walks toward the bench. The contact looks up.


EXT. PLAZA — LATE AFTERNOON

The contact exchange takes forty seconds. She does not watch the contact leave.

---

She is standing in the plaza with Jin against her right side and the sky above her and no next objective between her and the horizon. The contact has confirmed the route. There is a vehicle waiting two streets over. She knows where it is. She is going to walk to it.

She does not walk to it yet.

The air outside is different from the air inside. This is a fact about temperature and humidity and the specific properties of outdoor afternoon light in a city that is continuing its day. She notices it the way you notice a fact.

She does not let the notice become anything larger.

---

Jin looks up.

He has been looking at walls and ceilings and windows and the back of her shoulder for hours. He has been in enclosed spaces — the warm room, the corridor, the vehicle, the transit hub — and now the space above him has no ceiling. He looks at it. The light is different. The sky has the specific quality of late afternoon, going toward gold, going toward the longer shadows.

He looks at it for a while.

She lets him look.

---

She adjusts his weight — familiar geometry, right hip, left side managed — and begins walking toward the vehicle two streets over.

This adjustment is the same as every adjustment she has made since the room where she found him. The motion is identical. The rib is identical. The weight is identical.

The reason is different.

She is not managing toward the next obstacle. She is carrying him through open air in a city that does not know her name, toward a vehicle that will take them somewhere that is not VELTAIN.

What comes after that has not been solved. She is not solving it yet. She is walking.

---

Two streets. The vehicle. She opens the rear door and puts Jin in and closes it with care and gets in the front and does not look back at the plaza.

The future is not resolved. The ribs are not resolved. What she is is: outside. Moving. With him.

She drives.
