INT./EXT. ZORAL CORRIDOR — DECOMMISSIONED CHEMICAL TRANSIT DEPOT — NIGHT

The depot has been closed for eleven years. It was built to manage the transfer of industrial solvents between the rail freight line and the processing complex to the east, and when the processing complex closed the depot became what all such structures become: a long sequence of rust and residual hazard, marked at intervals with warning placards that nobody reads because nobody comes here.

NADIA has been moving through it for six minutes.

She knows she has company.

PIOTR GANZ moves through the lower tier of the pipe gallery with the unhurried precision of someone who has done this in worse conditions and does not need to rush because rushing is a tactic for people who might lose. He does not believe he will lose. The tracker relay on his wrist tells him she is forty-two meters ahead and moving north through the elevated section. He adjusts his approach accordingly.

The elevated section narrows to a single-file gallery at a junction of three converging pipe runs — she identified this when she entered. Single-file means his equipment advantage doesn't help him. She has thirty seconds to use this before he recalibrates.

She moves.

The junction is darker than the approach and smells of xylene residue — she registered the warning placard three meters back, the specific orange diamond with the vapor-hazard symbol, and noted it as usable. She does not need protection from residual xylene at this concentration. He will not know this. He will hesitate.

He does not hesitate.

He passes through the junction without breaking stride.

Twelve seconds.

She has been revising her model of him since contact. He is not improvising; he has operated in hazardous industrial environments before and he knows residue-level exposure is not immediate. He is working from training, not caution.

She reaches the pressure-relief valve on the main solvent run — a manual release, a large red wheel, corroded but operational. She has been moving toward this for three minutes. A forced release will produce a fog burst: pressurized vapor, non-toxic at this phase in the decommission cycle, but dense enough to reduce visibility to two meters for thirty to forty-five seconds.

She cranks the wheel.

The burst comes with a sound like a held breath releasing — a sustained hiss — and the fog rolls through the gallery in both directions.

She moves left. Away from the sound. Through the fog.

She hears him stop.

Then she hears him keep coming.

He is using the tracker relay. The fog does not matter to the relay. She has bought herself the time it takes him to cover twenty meters blind — perhaps fifteen seconds — and in those fifteen seconds she needs to reach the maintenance hatch at the gallery's north end and descend before the tracker locks her position accurately enough to close.

She runs.

Her ribs protest the running in a way that is no longer protest and more accurately described as structural notification: the fracture is responding to the lateral compression of her stride. She registers this as data — right lateral, worse than yesterday, running is borrowing against tomorrow — and keeps running.

The hatch is open. It was not open when she entered. She does not stop to consider what this means. She descends.

Lower tier. Drainage channels. Concrete floor, standing water at the edges, another pipe run along the south wall. She is now below and behind him.

She hears him descend after her. Thirty seconds behind. The fog has cleared from the upper gallery; the relay is sharp again.

She has no more prepared terrain.

She has the drainage channel, the pipe run, and the south wall, which ends in a dead-end bay that was used for chemical drum storage. She can see it from here. Forty meters. She calculates the time it will take him to close that forty meters after he reaches the lower tier.

She runs anyway, because the alternative is here.

She reaches the bay. It is what she knew it would be: a wall. Old drum cradles. Two HALO units mounted high on the south wall, their camera arrays rotating. The bay is a broadcast location. He will be careful here. Careful is not the same as slow.

She turns to face the entrance.

He appears at the far end of the drainage channel, fifty meters back, and walks toward her without hurrying.

PIOTR GANZ
Ms. Vale.

He does not raise his voice. The depot carries sound efficiently.

PIOTR GANZ (CONT'D)
There are two exits from the drainage level. Neither is behind you. I'd like you to consider the one on your left.

She does not look left. She already knows the left exit is a gated intake shaft that requires a key she does not have. He knows she knows this. The offer is not about the exit.

She inventories.

Her right side: the rib. Load-bearing on that side is unreliable. She cannot take a direct impact to the right torso and keep standing. Left side: functional. Forearm: she cut it on the sheared edge of the pipe run descent, she can feel the blood on her sleeve, she has not assessed the depth yet. The cut is a problem for later. Her hands are both operational. Her legs are her best asset.

He is thirty meters away.

Twenty.

She does not move. She is reading him — the way she was trained to read bodies, the way she read cadavers in her medic rotation before she read living patients: where the weight is distributed, where the skeletal structure commits to a direction before the surface gives it away. He is right-weight dominant. His gear is rear-heavy. When he closes to grapple range, his first move will be to his left.

Fifteen meters.

She moves right.

Not away — toward the drum cradles, and past them, using the cradle rail as a fulcrum to change her direction and come behind his right shoulder. It works for two seconds. His counter-rotation is fast. He catches her left forearm as she clears the rail and the grip is not a hold — it is a bar, a bar of force that pins the forearm against the cradle edge, and she hears the sound before she feels it: the shear of the pipe's corroded edge opening the skin of her forearm from elbow to mid-radius.

She uses his weight against itself — pivots into him, not away, forcing his grip to either hold and spin or release. He releases. She is past him. She has six meters.

The rib goes.

Not catastrophically — she does not go down. But the torque of the pivot has done what the running was doing incrementally: the fracture has displaced. She knows this because her right side has stopped being a resource and started being a liability. She can breathe. She cannot absorb impact on that side and remain standing.

She reaches the wall anyway.

The dead end.

He stands between her and the drainage channel. He has the restraint system ready. He is twelve meters away and not moving because he does not need to move. She has the wall behind her and her right side compromised and her forearm bleeding and six seconds before he decides she is done running.

He begins to close.

She presses her fingertips to her wrist — a brief, automatic gesture, checking the pulse: it is fast and functional. She is not done yet. She does not know what she is going to do.

Then, from somewhere in the lower depot — not close, but not far — a sound that is wrong for the space. Not the settling of old metal. Not the drip of standing water. Something that has intention in it. Movement with a direction.

Piotr Ganz stops.

He does not look away from her. But he stops. His head angles fractionally — listening — and she can see in the way his weight redistributes that he has just added a variable he did not have.

She watches him listen.

The sound comes again. Closer.

He looks.

The scene holds on her face — exhausted, bleeding, ribs broken, watching the entrance to the drum bay for whatever is coming — and the sounds of the depot in the dark, and whatever is moving through it toward them.
