INT. VELTAIN STAGING POSITION — SECONDARY RESIDENTIAL MAINTENANCE LEVEL — PRE-DAWN

The maintenance level runs beneath the secondary residential compound — utility access, pipe infrastructure, emergency lighting at floor level. Low ceiling. The sound of the building above is faint but continuous: ventilation, the building's ambient mechanical life.

Nadia and Zara have been here forty minutes.

Zara is sitting against the eastern wall, her back straight, her hand at her side below the jacket. The wound from the northern corridor is stable but no longer quiet — the forty minutes of stillness has allowed the body to register what the movement suppressed. She has said nothing about this.

Nadia is at the northern access point, a maintenance hatch that opens onto the service corridor above. She has checked it twice since they came down. It is the only exit they've confirmed is clear.

She returns to the center of the room and stops.

On the floor, propped against the base of the main utility conduit — a note. Folded once along a precise horizontal crease. Her name on the outside. Not her full name. Not her married name.

NADIA VALE

She did not put it there. It was not there when they arrived.

She picks it up. Unfolds it.

The note reads:

"Ms. Vale —

I understand that you are currently positioned within the secondary residential maintenance infrastructure. I have not communicated your position to your husband, and I do not intend to do so before we have had an opportunity to speak.

I am prepared to offer you a period of uninterrupted movement — twelve hours — during which my operational parameters will not include your immediate location. In exchange, I ask only that you contact me through the frequency attached before the twelve-hour window closes.

The frequency is: 143.225.

I am a professional and I understand the pressure you are operating under. I think a conversation would be mutually productive.

With respect,
P. GANZ"

She reads it once.

She reads it again. The second reading is slower.

She hands it to Zara without comment.

Zara reads it. Her face gives nothing. She hands it back.

ZARA
(very low)
He knows we're here.

NADIA
He's known since the service corridor.

ZARA
He didn't take it to Roman.

NADIA
He's telling us he didn't.

A pause. She looks at the note. The paper is good quality — the kind you do not find in a transit supply. The handwriting is even and unhurried, each letter formed without pressure variation. The pen did not skip. He did not write this quickly. He sat somewhere and wrote it carefully.

ZARA
The offer.

NADIA
Is a capture condition. Twelve hours of movement in exchange for a contact frequency. The moment we transmit, he has a fix. He probably already has a fix on the frequency — it's a receiver, not a two-way; any signal we send confirms position.

ZARA
So the offer is nothing.

NADIA
The offer is information. He's telling us he found us here before Roman did. He's telling us he's operating independently of the hunt parameters. He's telling us he prefers to work with time on his side.

She sets the note on the floor. Folds it along its existing crease — the precise horizontal fold, not her crease, his. Sets it back against the conduit base. Exactly where she found it.

ZARA
Why put it back.

NADIA
Because he'll know whether I did.

A moment. Zara looks at her.

NADIA (CONT'D)
He wins by outlasting. Every hunter in this city has been trying to contain me — force me into a corner, reduce the geometry. He's not doing that. He's extending the geometry. He wants time. Time he can work in.

She looks at her shoulder. Not at Zara — at her own shoulder, a brief inventory check, the kind that takes one second and reveals nothing to anyone watching who doesn't know what they're looking at.

Her shoulder is worse than it was at the ridge. The approach, the compound wall run, the conduit — forty minutes of stillness has done the same thing to it that it has done to Zara's wound. The body accounting for what it has been asked to absorb.

She looks away from her shoulder.

NADIA (CONT'D)
So we take the time away from him.

ZARA
How.

NADIA
We move before he expects us to move. We move before we're ready. We don't give him the window he's building.

ZARA
We're not ready.

NADIA
No.

She looks at the hatch. Looks at Zara.

NADIA (CONT'D)
How long.

ZARA
(a beat)
Give me twenty minutes.

NADIA
You have fifteen.

Zara accepts this. Begins removing the compress from her side with the efficiency of someone who has been through this process before, in other circumstances, and knows exactly what needs to happen next.

Nadia does not watch. She goes back to the hatch and checks it for the third time. The service corridor above is clear. The patrol interval puts the next circuit in eleven minutes.

Somewhere above them, the building breathes.

The note sits on the floor, folded along its crease, waiting.
