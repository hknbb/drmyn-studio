INT. EASTBOUND TRAIN — FORWARD-FACING SEAT — LATE MORNING

Fields moving outside the window. The tracks are smooth here. She has the device open in her lap, Marcus's documentation still scrolling through its sections.

She has been reading for forty minutes.

The section is labeled EVENT HISTORY — ORACLE PRIME. What it contains: a record of every previous iteration of the event. Seven events, spanning seven years. Marcus documented each one. Dates, event designations, duration, subject profile summary, final outcome.

She reads the final outcomes column.

All seven are the same word.

She stops.

She does not look at the window immediately. She stays with the screen for a moment — the kind of stillness that happens before the body catches up with the mind. Then she looks up. The fields are still moving. They have no relation to what she has read; they are simply fields. She looks at them anyway.

Seven events. Seven subjects. The audience data runs alongside the outcome records: ORACLE PRIME's viewership has grown with every event. The machine's perfect record is also its reputation. People return because it always ends the same way. The event's duration varies — some subjects lasted hours, one lasted three days — but the outcome is invariant. The audience has watched seven people die and come back each time with more subscribers.

She is the current broadcast.

She understands this. She has understood it since the beginning in the abstract. The abstract version was something she could work alongside. This version — seven names, seven outcomes, seven audience metrics that each exceeded the previous — this version is something else.

She holds the device without looking at it.

The fields are moving outside. Birch trees now, pale and regular, passing too fast to count. She watches them without watching them.

This is what it is. Seven events, seven deaths, a business that has operated without failure for seven years. She is the eighth subject. There is no outcome record yet for the eighth event because the eighth event is ongoing. She is on a train, which is a kind of forward motion, which is something she chose, which is not nothing.

She does not argue with the statistical reality. She has had enough conversations with herself to know that arguing with a complete record is a form of denial that costs more than it buys. The record is what it is. She holds it.

Then, because this is what she does when she holds something true, she looks at what is actually different.

Marcus's documentation is three hundred and forty pages. The previous subjects received no documentation. She knows this because Marcus's documentation says so — he searched the event history for any subject who had advanced intelligence going into the event. None. They knew they were hunted; none of them knew by what; none of them knew the financial architecture under the broadcast; none of them had a named target.

She has a named target.

Marcus built this for someone who would come after him. He failed; he knew he might fail; he documented anyway, with the precision of someone who understood that the documentation would only matter if its recipient survived long enough to use it. He built a weapon and died before he could deploy it. He left it for whoever came next.

She is whoever came next.

The train passes through a small station without stopping. A platform, empty, gone in three seconds. She watches it disappear.

The previous subjects were not less capable than her. This is important to hold clearly: she should not inflate the variable into something it is not. They were capable, intelligent, fighting. They simply did not have what she has. One variable — not capability, not character, not luck. One structural difference: she knows the target and the mechanism. The broadcast architecture. Destroy it and the routing collapses and Roman's machine loses its financial infrastructure and the event dies.

That is the variable.

The knowledge is not certainty. She writes this down in her head the way she would write it in a brief — as a fact that constrains the claim, not as a limit on the action. Knowing the target does not guarantee reaching it. Reaching it does not guarantee deploying the mechanism. Deploying the mechanism does not guarantee that nothing goes wrong. It is the difference between having a plan with a valid target and having a plan that succeeds. Those are not the same thing.

Seven is still the number. One variable different is still what she has.

She looks at the device again.

Marcus's documentation has a section on Roman's private archive — the record is specific enough to be sourced rather than speculative. Roman preserves items from every event. He has built a private collection, housed at his residence inside the VELTAIN facility, organized by event and date. It is not described as a trophy room in the documentation; it is described as a collection. The distinction is Roman's, not Marcus's. She reads the description twice.

Seven events. Seven additions to the collection. Each one is an outcome, preserved.

She understands that she is looking at the shape of what she will need to enter.

She closes the event history section and returns to where she was: the broadcast architecture files. The relay installation diagrams. UNIT 7-ECHO.

The train moves east.

She keeps reading.
