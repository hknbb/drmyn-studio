INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — UPPER CORRIDOR — NIGHT

The upper level of Roman's building is not the operational level — no server rooms, no security stations, no broadcast infrastructure. It is residential in scale: carpeted, climate-controlled, the ambient temperature of a room that exists to be comfortable. Nadia's footsteps are quieter here than they have been anywhere in the building.

She finds the door Marcus described. Third on the left from the elevator landing. Unmarked. A standard interior lock — residential, not security-grade. Roman did not lock this room because he did not expect anyone to find it who did not already know what was in it.

She opens it.

INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — THE TROPHY ROOM — CONTINUOUS

Silence first. The specific quality of a sealed room — no ventilation noise, no ambient hum, just the presence of air that has been still for a long time.

She steps in and stops.

The room is approximately four meters by five. Three walls are fitted with glass-fronted display cases, floor to ceiling, the kind used in private museums — dust-controlled, humidity-managed, lit from within by narrow strip lighting that casts each case's contents in a careful, even glow. The fourth wall holds a single flat panel displaying a formatted document.

She reads the panel first.

FORMATTED DOCUMENT — WALL PANEL:

[EVENT LOG — ORACLE PRIME PRIVATE OPERATIONS — EVENTS 1 THROUGH 14]

Fourteen events. Dates spanning nine years. Each entry: an event designation, a location code, a duration in hours and days, and a single-word outcome field. The outcome field for every entry — all fourteen — reads: CLOSED.

She knows what closed means. She has known since Marcus's package. But knowing and seeing are different processes, and she gives herself the two seconds it takes to convert the knowledge into the specific weight of confirmed fact.

Fourteen events. Fourteen outcomes.

She turns to the cases.

The cases contain personal effects. Not weapons, not evidence in any forensic sense — objects that were on people when they stopped being people the game needed to track. A pair of reading glasses, one lens cracked. A child's drawing, folded in quarters, the paper yellowed. A wedding ring in a small labeled tray. A hotel room key card from a city that no longer uses key cards. A medical bracelet — not Jin's; an older model; someone else's. A watch, stopped.

The objects are arranged with care. Each has a small label: event number, date. No names. Roman did not keep their names. He kept their things.

She reads the cases the way she reads the room: systematic. She is looking for Marcus's reference point. In his intelligence package — assembled over two years of working inside Roman's outer network — he described a specific object he had confirmed was in this room. He had not been here himself. He had the description from an intermediary who had been here once, years ago, and who had since disappeared.

She finds it in the third case from the left.

A small analog clock. The kind that used to sit on a desk. Its face is printed with the name of a media company that no longer exists — the first target organization Roman dismantled before he moved to private individuals. The clock's hands are stopped at 11:43. Marcus noted the stopped time in his package without comment. He had no reason to comment. Neither does she.

She has her confirmation.

She stands still for a moment in this room that has held fourteen endings. Not long — the clock in her mind, the one she has been running since the service corridor, is still counting. Roman's technical team will be reconfiguring the security response. Every minute she spends standing is a minute she does not have.

But she stays for one full beat, because the room requires it. Because it would be wrong to be in this room and not register what it is.

Fourteen people who did not come back.

She is the fifteenth.

She turns to the fourth case — positioned slightly apart from the others, at the far end of the room, facing the door as if Roman placed it to be found last by anyone who got this far. Which no one ever did.

The case contains a single item: a sealed data card in a clear holder. Small, black, unremarkable. The card's label reads: SYSTEM TERMINATION — ORACLE PRIME BROADCAST NETWORK — AUTHORIZED ADMINISTRATOR USE ONLY.

The case's latch has a factory seal. It is intact. The seal has never been broken.

She looks at it for the length of one breath.

She breaks the seal and opens the case.

The card is in her hand.

She looks at it — small, light, ordinary. The kind of thing that should not be able to do what it will do. Marcus had described it in his package based on the same intermediary's account. He had written: "If it exists, it will be in the room. If you reach the room, you already know what to do with it."

She puts the card in her jacket pocket.

She does not look at the room again as she leaves.
