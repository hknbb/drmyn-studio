EXT. MERIN OUTER SERVICE DISTRICT — PRE-DAWN

The freight corridor ends at a service gate. She goes through it at a run and the city opens.

MERIN. The outer service district: loading bays, relay infrastructure, the underside of a city that does not know it is being used as a hunting ground. The sky is beginning to grey at the eastern margin.

She has approximately ninety seconds before ORACLE PRIME's coverage picks her up.

She does not have ninety seconds.

The broadcast was dark on her for five hours and seventeen minutes — the dead zone, the safe house, the time the audience spent watching odds that did not move. The return of coverage triggers immediately: she can see the camera architecture on the relay towers as she moves, and she can feel the betting activity resuming the way you feel surveillance resume — not evidence, but a shift in the air's attention.

She goes underground.

The freight corridor maps she built from Marcus's intelligence package gave her the MERIN service infrastructure: loading access routes that run below street level between the outer district and the middle quarter. Civilian vehicles cannot use them. She can.

Two minutes in the first loading tunnel. She emerges on the wrong side of a street and has to cross.

She crosses.

Thirty meters of open street. A hunter vehicle — she clocks it on the right margin: stationary, engine off, two occupants reading the broadcast feed on a mounted screen. They see her on the broadcast before they see her in person. She is already back into the service corridor on the far side before they can move.

The broadcast is giving her position to people who can then move to intercept. This is the coordination mechanism. She is not outrunning the hunters; she is outrunning the speed of their response to the broadcast data.

Middle quarter. The service routes become less clean here — mixed access, civilian and freight overlap, she has to read each junction before she moves through it.

She reads them. She moves.

The left knee is marking every sixth or seventh step with a specific pain that is different from impact pain and she has been in enough situations to know the difference: the joint is compromised. The loading dock landing was a two-floor drop to a hard surface with lateral torque. She landed and ran. The suture on the penetration wound is holding — she can feel it holding, Tobias's work, the careful layering she felt even through the lidocaine last night — but the running is testing it.

She does not think about Tobias.

She runs.

Three more junctions. A hunter on foot in the middle quarter — she sees him before he sees her, reads his position from his orientation to the nearest camera mount, and goes left instead of straight. She loses forty seconds. She accepts it.

The waterfront perimeter is two blocks east.

The left knee gives on the step off a loading platform edge — not a collapse, a yield, a warning. She catches it, adjusts the gait, moves at reduced speed for four steps before she can push back to pace. The joint is telling her something she cannot afford to hear yet.

One block east.

The utility corridor entrance: a maintenance hatch in the waterfront approach road, iron cover, not locked from outside, the kind of access point that exists on city infrastructure maps and nowhere else. She found it in the intelligence package. She finds it now by the second intersection marker from the approach road junction.

She goes down.

INT. UTILITY CORRIDOR — WATERFRONT APPROACH — DAWN

Concrete. Low ceiling. The sound of the city above her becomes a muffled pressure. No cameras. No broadcast coverage. She is off the feed again, but this time by geography rather than design — this is the accidental dead zone of a service corridor, not Roman's captivity architecture.

She stops running.

The left knee registers the transition from running to standing with a spike of specific information. Compound fracture risk: she can categorize it now, in stillness, the way a medic categorizes an injury report. The joint is structurally compromised. She can move. She cannot run.

Above her, the hunters are at the hatch. She hears them: two sets of footfall, a vehicle, the sound of a cover being tried. The hatch is too narrow for the response configuration they arrived in. They will find another way into the corridor system but they do not know this particular route and they will lose time finding it.

She is barely free.

She begins to move at a controlled pace through the corridor toward the waterfront.

She does not think about Tobias.

She does not stop.


EXT. MERIN WATERFRONT — DOCK PERIMETER — DAWN

The maintenance passage ends at a steel door. Beyond it: the waterfront.

She pushes through. Cold air off the water. The light is low and horizontal and the dock is in the long morning shadow of the cold-store facility — a freight-handling complex, pre-shift, the kind of quiet that has personnel somewhere inside it but not visible, not yet. She reads the perimeter as she moves. Service access gates, equipment staging areas, the outer dock wall where the freight pallets are stacked three meters high and the shadow runs deep.

She does not go toward the water. She goes toward the wall.

The equipment shelter is unlocked: a wooden structure leaned against the cold-store outer face, the kind of thing that accumulates on working docks without formal designation — tool storage, pallet overflow, the residue of operational habit. Freight pallets stacked on two sides. The dock wall on the third. The shelter on the fourth. The shadow from the cold-store roof covers the entrance.

She goes in and stops.
