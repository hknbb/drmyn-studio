INT. OUTER DISTRICTS — COVERED INTERIOR COURTYARD — MORNING

She comes in low through the gap in the eastern wall. The courtyard is roofed on two sides — corrugated sheeting, structurally adequate. No sight lines to the main access route. Standpipe mounted to the interior wall, north-east corner: she found it during the second mapping sweep, filed it under contingency.

This is the contingency.

She closes the gap behind her with a section of loose sheeting. Not locked. Three seconds to move if she has to.

---

The sleeve is already dark to the cuff. She unbuttons it, rolls it back.

The wound from here: draw cut, outside edge of the left forearm. Full length, clean edges — a sharp knife. She notes that. A sharp knife is a professional's instrument, not a blade carried for intimidation. It recalibrates Solv upward. He was already senior. The knife confirms it.

She turns the standpipe valve. The water runs brown for four seconds, then clears. She holds the forearm under it.

The cleaning takes ninety seconds. She does not rush it. Cold water, no antiseptic, acceptable trade-off: the wound is recent, the edges are clean, the risk of immediate contamination is lower than the risk of leaving debris in the wound bed. She was taught this in a field setting considerably worse than this courtyard.

She tears a strip from the inner hem of her shirt — approximately four centimeters wide, thirty centimeters long. She has done this before. Not exactly this, but the category of this.

---

The adrenaline crash arrives while she is applying pressure to the initial fold.

She categorizes it: sympathetic nervous system rebound. Thirty to sixty seconds. It will peak and pass. Her hands do not stop.

What peaks: her heart rate runs briefly erratic, her vision sharpens at the edges in the specific, useless way it does when the body decides the threat is over before the brain has confirmed it, a wave of something that is not quite nausea. She breathes through it. Four counts in, four counts out — a number her training instructor gave her once and that she has never stopped using.

She does not think about Solv while her hands are occupied.

The crash passes. Her hands have not stopped.

---

She applies the dressing in layers: pressure, fold, bind. She uses the wire from her pocket — the same wire she carried into the drop — to secure the outer wrap. Not elegant. Functional.

She ties it off left-handed, which requires more care than she would prefer. Acceptable.

She holds the arm still for thirty seconds. Then she closes her hand.

Full grip. Three times.

The dressing holds. The bleeding has stopped or slowed to a manageable rate. She cannot tell which without removing the dressing and she is not going to remove the dressing.

---

She rolls the sleeve back down. Buttons the cuff.

The wire shows at the wrist — a small protrusion under the fabric. She adjusts the sleeve to cover it. Not perfect. It will pass at a glance.

She has been in this courtyard for approximately eight minutes. That is longer than she wanted. She turns the standpipe valve closed, checks the courtyard floor — no visible blood pool, the water from the cleaning has spread and is drying on the concrete — and moves to the gap in the eastern wall.

She takes one moment before she goes through.

The encounter, filed. Solv's capability, updated. The wound, managed. The dressing will hold for three to four hours before it needs attention. She has three to four hours.

She goes through the gap and does not look back.


EXT./INT. ORACLE PRIME BROADCAST LAYER / KESTON LALE'S APARTMENT — EVENING

We have a first contact report. Event Cycle 114. The subject engaged a Tier-2 contracted position in the outer districts — approximately fifty-two minutes after launch. Field contact was brief. Resolution: withdrawal with minor injury to the subject, hunter position disengaged. No termination.
