INT. COLD-STORE EQUIPMENT SHELTER — MERIN WATERFRONT — CONTINUOUS

She sits down against the dock wall with her back to the concrete and her legs extended and she does not move for thirty seconds.

Then she begins the inventory.

The penetration wound first. She has been running for forty-seven minutes since Tobias sutured it. She unzips the jacket, finds the dressing — intact — and presses the surrounding tissue with two fingers in the pattern she was trained to use: proximal first, then distal, then the wound margins directly. She is looking for warmth. For swelling that is disproportionate to the impact. For any sign of exudate at the dressing edge.

She finds warmth at the margin.

Not much. Not the diffuse heat of established infection. Focal, at the edge of where the suture line runs, the specific quality of tissue that is deciding. The window is still open. She cannot tell for how long.

She holds the assessment for a moment.

His sutures are eight in count. Interrupted technique — she can feel the individual placement through the dressing, each one placed with the economy of someone who sutured under field conditions enough times to make the motion automatic. She did not watch him work. She felt it. She feels it now, her fingers reading what he made.

She does not stop the inventory.

The left knee. She straightens the leg fully and rotates the foot — medial, then lateral — reading the joint's response to each axis. The instability is medial and the movement that produces it is the same movement that failed on the loading platform edge: weight transfer under lateral torque. She can categorize it now in stillness. Compression impact, two-floor drop, the landing was not level, the knee absorbed the rotational difference. The cartilage is involved. She cannot confirm this without imaging but she has enough anatomy to name the mechanism and enough field experience to know what the mechanism means.

She can bear weight. She cannot run.

She accepts this and files it as a constraint.

Beyond the shelter entrance, the dock is coming to life: a motor somewhere, the sound of freight handling beginning in the cold-store complex, a voice in the middle distance saying something procedural to someone else. None of it directed toward her. The pre-shift activity is its own system; she is outside it, in the residue space where dock workers put things they are not currently using.

She is temporarily hidden.

She understands the difference between this and safe. She has been understanding this difference for eleven days.

She leans her head back against the dock wall and looks at the underside of the shelter roof and allows herself, for the first time since the alley outside 14 Havel Court, to feel the weight of the last eight hours without managing it.

It does not feel like anything she expected.

It feels like the dock. The cold. The horizontal light coming through the gap in the shelter entrance. The smell of the water and the freight and the industrial cold of the storage facility. The weight of the wound and the knee and the forty-seven minutes of running on both of them. The eight sutures she can feel through the dressing.

She does not cry. There is nothing inside her available for that.

She sits with it for one breath. Then she begins to plan.


INT. KESTON'S APARTMENT — EARLY MORNING

The notification arrives at 6:14 a.m. He has been asleep for two hours.

He reaches for the phone before he is fully awake. The broadcast icon. Coverage resumed. He unlocks the screen and THE HERALD is already mid-report, in the warm register he associates with a good stretch of the event — the voice slightly brightened, the delivery running fast, the tone of a commentator who has good news to give.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
— and what we're looking at now is the full sequence, for anyone who came in after the coverage gap. Five hours and seventeen minutes of restricted coverage, which, I have to say, created some of the most sustained anticipation this event has generated since day four. And then this.

He watches on the phone. He does not get out of bed. He does not move to the television.

The broadcast runs the MERIN chase footage from ORACLE PRIME's cameras: the outer service district, the coverage resumption, the loading corridor emergence, the middle quarter street crossings. The hunt. The Herald has edited the sequence for maximum impact — the moments where the hunter vehicles were closest, the brief street exposures, the deteriorating run in the final blocks before the waterfront approach.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
Now, here's what's interesting — the audience response when coverage resumed was the highest single-minute betting volume this event has recorded. Our audience was waiting. They knew something was coming. And what came was — well. You're watching it. Eleven days in, and she is still running.

He watches. He is watching the way he always watches: at a slight remove, the consumer register, the part of his attention that tracks narrative and tension and the mechanics of the production. The editing is good. The camera architecture in the service district is impressive — the coverage continuity through below-street-level routes, the way ORACLE PRIME manages its positions. He notices this. It is easier to notice this.

The broadcast reaches the close call: the final blocks before the waterfront approach, the deteriorating gait visible in the footage, the left leg marking every few steps with a compensatory movement that the camera captures in precise detail.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
The field assessment on the contestant's current physical status puts the left leg at significant risk after this sequence. Compound fracture probability is in the range that our medical commentary team has flagged as meaningful. What that means for the event's timeline — well. The odds have adjusted.

He turns the volume down slightly. Then back up. He is not sure why he turned it down.

KESTON
It's not like —

He stops.

He watches the footage of the deteriorating run. The left leg. Every few steps. The camera holds on it with the patience of a system that has all the time available to it that the broadcast requires.

KESTON
It's not like this is —

He watches.

He changes the subject to the production. The camera architecture is genuinely impressive. The way ORACLE PRIME routes its coverage through below-street infrastructure — you don't get that kind of continuity from a conventional broadcast rig. The editing decision to hold on the gait deterioration is probably an algorithm call, not a human one. He thinks about this. He thinks about whether that makes it different.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
Current odds are sitting at eight to one for a Day Twelve resolution. Our audience has been generous with their support of this position. For those of you who came in on the early odds — you've had a good eleven days.

He turns the broadcast off.

He does not throw the phone. He does not make a decision about what the turn-off means. He sets the phone face-down on the table next to the bed, screen against the wood.

The apartment is quiet.

The broadcast is still running somewhere — on other screens, on other phones, in other apartments in this city and other cities. The betting volume is at its highest single-session figure. The Herald is continuing the close call analysis with the same warm precision it brings to everything. His bet is still active. His participation in the event's financial structure has not changed.

He sits in the quiet and does not open the phone.

He sits for a long time.
