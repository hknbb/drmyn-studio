INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — INTERNAL CORRIDOR / SERVICE DESCENT — CONTINUOUS

She is already moving.

The compound's lockdown is Dimitri's coup protocol — automated, not human, triggered when the financial seizure went active. She knew this from the documentation: the protocol was designed for a specific threat model (external intervention, not internal) and it sequences doors in a predictable order. The documentation was from Morrow. She read it in the trophy room while she was looking at the closed event files. She has the sequence.

First tier: main entry and broadcast levels. Sealed. Second tier: administrative and server core access. Sealed — she came through the gap before it closed. Third tier: residential and compound level access. Thirty-seven minutes from trigger to full seal.

The trigger was 13:41.

She has been in the building for forty-one minutes.

She is four minutes behind the seal on the third-tier doors. There is one route through — the service descent, a stairwell that runs parallel to the administrative shaft, rated for maintenance use only, not on the automated protocol because it was not on the original floor plan submitted to the authority that approved Dimitri's lockdown specification. Morrow noted the omission in a marginal comment dated fourteen months ago.

She has been moving toward it since she left the antechamber.

The bracelet reader has been giving her intermittent signal in the upper corridors. The signal is a passive ID chip — the hospital bracelet she registered Jin with at eight weeks, the one he was wearing when they took him. The chip does not transmit; it reflects. To read it she needs line of sight through no more than three intervening surfaces with a signal density below a specific threshold.

In the upper corridors, there are too many intervening surfaces. The signal comes in fragments.

In the service descent stairwell, which runs along the compound's eastern structural shaft, there are two intervening surfaces between the reader and compound level B.

The signal changes.

PASSIVE ID: VALE, JIN — COMPOUND LEVEL B
BEARING 247° — SIGNAL STRENGTH: STRONG
LAST CONFIRMED: 00:04:12 AGO

She stops walking for one second.

The signal is not Jin. She knows this — the chip does not give temperature or movement or any of the things she wants to know. It gives location. It gives bearing. It gives strength, which is a proxy for proximity and intervening material, not for safety.

The signal is strong. The bearing is specific. He is sixty-three meters below her and eleven meters to the southwest, which places him in compound level B's eastern residential section, which she has in the floor plan, which she can reach through this stairwell in four minutes at her current pace.

She does not stop again.

The service descent door at the bottom landing is mid-state — resting against the frame, no current through the electromagnetic lock. Someone was in the middle of closing it when the coup alert recalled them. She can see the small indicator light on the lock housing showing no active current: amber, not red. Not sealed.

She pushes it open.

The corridor to compound level B is ahead of her. The signal is strong. The distance is known. She has four minutes of margin before the tier-three seal completes and she is past the last door she needs before the seal matters.

She moves.


INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — COMPOUND LEVEL B, EASTERN RESIDENTIAL ROOM — CONTINUOUS

The room is small and warm and lit by a lamp that someone chose for its quality of light rather than its output — a care choice, not a facility choice. There is a crib against the far wall. There is a chair beside it. There is Nurse Irina, who has been in this room for eleven days and who is standing now with her hands at her sides because she heard the building change an hour ago and she has been waiting to understand what that meant.

She understands it now.

Nadia is in the doorway. She is wearing what she has been wearing through the compound. Her left side is compensating for something. Her face is the face of a person who has arrived at the end of a very long and costly distance.

Nurse Irina steps back. She does not reach for anything. She does not say anything. She moves away from the crib and toward the wall and she stays there.

Jin is awake.

He is eight months old and he does not know what has happened. He does not know that the building around him has been a controlled environment inside a predator machine, or that the machine is gone, or that the woman in the doorway has been moving toward this room since before he was taken. He is eight months old. He is looking at the ceiling fan, which he has been looking at with interest since it was first switched on six days ago. It is his current object of attention.

Nadia crosses the room.

She does not rush. She has been running on emergency reserve for forty-one minutes and she does not need to run now. She walks to the crib and she looks at him for one second — the specific inventory she has been carrying: the scar at the left ear (birth mark, crescent-shaped, three millimeters); the fingers already curled around nothing in the habitual grip she knows. He is unchanged. He is exactly himself.

She picks him up.

He startles slightly at the transfer — the shift from surface to held — and then settles. He is not distressed. He does not know her from any other person who has held him in this room. He does not know he should be relieved. He settles because being held is a known condition and she is holding him with the specific competence of a person who knows how he settles.

On his wrist: the hospital bracelet. The white band with the small dark reader chip she tracked across three security zones and two levels of a locked-down building. It is on him. It was always on him.

She does not look at it long.

Nurse Irina, from the wall: "He's been fed. An hour ago."

Nadia does not respond to this with words. She nods once — acknowledgment of information, not dismissal of the person who gave it. It is the kind of nod that says: I heard you. I am registering the practical content of what you said. Thank you.

She holds him.

Her left side is what it is. The rib injury is categorical and has been categorical since the antechamber corridor and it will remain categorical for the foreseeable future. She is holding her baby and her left side is what it is and both of those things are true at the same time in this small warm room and neither of them is the story she will tell later, if she tells one. The story she will tell later is the one that starts: I went to get him and I got him.

The ceiling fan turns.

Jin looks at it.
