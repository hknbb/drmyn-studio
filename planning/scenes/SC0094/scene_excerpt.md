EXT. VELTAIN INNER DISTRICT — SERVICE CORRIDOR — PRE-DAWN

The service corridor runs east between the compound walls. At its far end — two hundred meters — the secondary residential compound. Its eastern face. Four stories. The lighting on this side is functional: security lamps at the roofline, angled down. Below the lamp threshold, the lower wall is in shadow.

Nadia walks east. Controlled pace. The service corridor is empty at this hour — the patrol interval she mapped from the ridge puts the nearest circuit eight minutes away.

She is counting down from eight.

She reaches the corridor's end where it meets the compound's outer service access — a setback in the wall, a maintenance recess two meters deep. She steps into it. Presses her back against the wall. The lamp above is angled past her. She is in the shadow band.

She takes the device from the medical kit.

It is not a tracker. It is a hospital ID reader — the kind used to pull a patient's registration from a passive NFC bracelet at close range. She pulled it from the cache in the outer transit hub, listed in the deployment documents' medical inventory as a standard triage aid. At clinical range — one meter — it reads the bracelet's chip with certainty. At six meters, it struggles. At twelve, it produces noise.

The compound wall is three meters away.

She turns the reader on. The indicator light is blue: scanning. She holds it toward the wall at chest height and waits.

Nothing.

She takes one step toward the wall. Two. The indicator pulses — irregular, searching. The interference from the wall is producing scatter in the read. She needs to be closer.

She looks at the corner of the service recess. The maintenance panel — the access point that brought them into the inner district — is three meters to her left. The compound's exterior patrol circuit rounds the building's northern corner and passes the access point at its midpoint. If the patrol is on schedule, she has six minutes.

She steps out of the recess. Walks directly to the compound wall.

She is eight meters from the building. The indicator pulses faster — it has found something. A weak signal, intermittent, breaking up against the wall's concrete and whatever signal management the compound's internal infrastructure produces.

She moves along the wall. North, toward the corner. The signal grows as she walks. A bracelet with a passive chip does not transmit — it responds to a reader within range. Which means the reader needs to be close to Jin.

Which means Jin is somewhere in the direction she is walking.

She counts seven paces north. The indicator changes — the pulse steadies, no longer searching. It has established a read.

She stops. Looks at the display.

The bracelet ID is there. His hospital registration number — the one she filled out herself, in the MERIN clinic, the morning she noticed the bruise on his left shin from the cot rail and asked the nurse to note it and the nurse said of course and she said thank you and she meant it and she went home and Roman was at the table having breakfast. This number. Here.

She does not move for one breath.

Then she moves.

The display gives a signal quality reading and a relative bearing. The signal is coming from inside the building, elevated — two floors above the ground level, based on the vertical attenuation. The northern end of the east wing. The deployment documents give the secondary residential compound's internal layout as four residential levels with service access at the central core. Second residential level, northern corner.

She has been studying this floor plan for three days. She knows which room is in the northern corner of the second level. She knows it has one window facing north and one facing east. She knows the east-facing window is visible from the outer service corridor on a clear line.

She knows which window is his.

She maps the bearing against the floor plan. The bearing matches. She checks it twice — not from uncertainty, from the discipline that does not allow single-point confirmation for something this consequential. The second check matches the first.

She looks up at the building.

The window is dark.

He is in there. The window is dark because it is four hours before dawn and he is asleep. He is asleep in a room with two windows. He has a caretaker — the deployment documents list a NURSE IRINA on residential duty — and he is safe in the way that things are safe when the threat has not yet moved to its final stage.

She stands in the exterior patrol circuit's sightline, eight meters from the compound wall, in the pre-dawn dark, and she knows exactly where her son is.

The indicator on the reader continues to pulse. She turns it off.

A sound — the patrol circuit. The northern corner, thirty seconds early. Her count was wrong by thirty seconds.

She moves back to the service recess. Presses into the shadow band. The patrol passes on the compound wall's other side — a guard with a flashlight that sweeps the outer face at head height, methodical, covering the lamp-shadow band. The beam reaches the maintenance recess, tracks across it, moves on.

The guard does not stop.

She waits until the footsteps recede past the service corridor's entrance. Counts twenty more seconds after that.

She moves back into the service corridor. West, back toward the staging position where Zara is waiting.

Her face gives nothing. Her pace has not changed. The device is back in the medical kit.

The only evidence of what happened is the information she is carrying — precise, confirmed, mapped against a floor plan she has memorized — and the one breath she stood still.
