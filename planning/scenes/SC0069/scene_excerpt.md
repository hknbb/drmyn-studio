EXT. KASPAR TERMINAL — LOADING BAY BREAK AREA — AFTERNOON

The eastern loading bays. A working area — pallets stacked along the terminal wall, a freight scale, a standpipe with a tap at knee height. A service door propped open with a rubber wedge. The kind of gap in a working facility that exists because someone needed a shortcut and no one closed it after.

She came in through the service door.

She clocked the cameras on approach: one above the terminal's main entry arch, one on the corner of the freight office building, one — and this one she almost missed — above the loading bay door, in the gap between the bay's overhang and the wall. It points down and to the right. It covers the tap.

She uses the tap anyway. She needs the water.

She fills her palms and drinks four times. She fills them again and holds them over the back of her neck — the thermal management her training specifies for elevated core temperature. She does this for thirty seconds.

NESSA is on a pallet near the wall, twenty feet away, eating from a paper bag. Late thirties. Work jacket, waterproof trousers, the kind of boots that have seen several winters. She is not paying particular attention to Nadia.

Then she is.

She looks at her the way a practical person looks at something that is not quite right — not alarmed, not curious. The specific look of someone taking stock.

NESSA
You look like you've been out since yesterday.

It is not a question.

NADIA
Overnight shift.

NESSA
Where.

NADIA
Freight handling. North gate.

Nessa looks at her for a moment. She is not convinced, but she is not particularly interested in the gap between what Nadia said and what she looks like. She reaches into the paper bag and takes out half a bread roll, wrapped in a paper napkin. She holds it out.

NESSA
Here.

Nadia takes it.

NESSA (CONT'D)
Staff room's through the service door, down the corridor on
the left. There's a kettle. They don't lock it during the
day.

She takes a bite of her own food and looks out at the freight yard. The exchange is over, in her register — she has addressed what was in front of her.

NADIA
The security sweep.

NESSA
Four o'clock. They come through the bays first.

She says this without particular significance — the same information she would give anyone who needed it. She does not assign meaning to the question.

She goes back to her lunch.

Nadia moves toward the service door. She carries the bread roll in the hand away from the wall — the hand that is not near the wound. She is managing the gait modification for the knee: the step slightly shortened on the left, the weight transfer adjusted. She has been doing this long enough that it is close to invisible if you are not looking for it.

She goes through the service door.

She does not look at the camera above the loading bay door.

She knows it was there. She knows it was running. She does not know if the algorithm flagged the exchange — a stationary person and a moving person in a monitored area, a brief transfer of material. She files the uncertainty as a known exposure and continues.

INT. KASPAR TERMINAL — STAFF CORRIDOR — CONTINUOUS

The corridor is narrow and functional. Strip lighting. A board with shift schedules pinned to it. The staff room door is third on the left.

She goes in. A table, chairs, a kettle, a small refrigerator. A window with a view of a wall. No camera she can find.

She fills the kettle and sits down.

She eats the bread roll.

Outside, through the corridor, through the loading bay, the camera above the door is still running. Nessa has finished her lunch and gone back to work. The camera has no record of what either of them thought about the exchange. It has a record of the exchange.
