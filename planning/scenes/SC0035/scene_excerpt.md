EXT. INDUSTRIAL APPROACH — ZORAL CORRIDOR FRINGE — LATE AFTERNOON

The light here is different. Grey-orange industrial haze, not the urban amber of VARDOVA. Prefabricated processing sheds line the access road in both directions — corrugated metal, company identifiers stenciled in Cyrillic and a second language. Surveillance cameras on every fourth post. Not civilian cameras. These are the ones that record.

NADIA moves through the approach lane on the pedestrian margin, worker-side, following the logic of the space. She is carrying a transit worker's jacket she picked up three blocks back — not clean, not a costume, just a layer that fits the register of this space. She has been moving for hours. Her left foot comes down slightly wrong every fourth step. She corrects it each time. The correction costs something.

A HALO UNIT drifts at middle height over the processing lane. Its camera array is indexed toward the vehicle flow. She does not look at it. Looking is data.

She reaches a gap between two sheds and moves into the shadow.

She stops.

She takes inventory. Right hand presses briefly to the inside of her left wrist. Real number. Acceptable. She notes the camera post angles. She traces the sight-lines.

The Corridor checkpoint is visible from here — two hundred meters. The booth is lit. CAPTAIN DRUNE's jurisdiction. She can't see anyone in the booth from this angle but the booth is always staffed when the booth is lit.

She is thinking through her options when the shadow across from her moves.

LOTTI steps out from the loading dock recess. Forties. Practical clothing, no insignia. A bag over one shoulder. She is already checking her watch as she speaks.

LOTTI
You're late. I was told one.

She looks at Nadia with the flat assessment of someone who schedules living things and does not appreciate when they run behind.

LOTTI (CONT'D)
Here's where I land on this — I give the booth window twenty minutes from now, and twenty minutes started six minutes ago.

Nadia does not answer immediately. She reads Lotti. She reads the dock bay behind her. She reads the checkpoint sight-line from this new angle.

A woman with a schedule. A border crossing already arranged. A route through the booth that bypasses the camera array on the north post — which is the array wired into Drune's feed.

NADIA
One.

LOTTI
(checks her watch)
Right. Come on, then.

She moves back toward the dock bay. She adjusts the bag on her shoulder. She does not check if Nadia is following — she is already recalculating timing.

Nadia follows.

The dock bay opens onto a service alley that runs parallel to the pedestrian margin for sixty meters before it bends toward the checkpoint's secondary gate — the vehicle-adjacent lane. Less camera coverage. Staff entrance logic rather than public crossing.

LOTTI
You have documents?

NADIA
Yes.

LOTTI
Fold them. Back pocket. Staff don't open a back-pocket document. They're expecting personnel, not a pocket.

She says this without looking back. It is operational instruction, not suggestion.

NADIA
Understood.

LOTTI
And don't favor your left side when we're in sight of the booth. Walk even.

Nadia absorbs this. Lotti clocked the gait. She said it once. She is already done with it.

LOTTI (CONT'D)
Here's where I land on this — you make it across, you're done with me. I don't run return trips. Whatever you're moving toward, you're moving toward alone.

NADIA
That's fine.

LOTTI
(checking her watch again)
Fourteen minutes.

They move through the alley. The industrial sounds of the Corridor close around them — distant machinery, the rattle of a transport gate, the ambient hum of the surveillance infrastructure. The checkpoint booth is ahead. The light inside it is constant and indifferent.

Nadia walks even.
