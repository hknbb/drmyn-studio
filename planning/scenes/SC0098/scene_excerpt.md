EXT./INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — OUTER PERIMETER — NIGHT

The VELTAIN compound from the outside is not a compound. It is a building in a row of buildings — a financial district address, the kind of address that costs more than most people earn in a decade. The security is invisible by design. Hilda Krast believes that visible security announces that the thing being protected is worth protecting, which tells the wrong people where to look. The security at VELTAIN is structural. It is built into the architecture itself.

Nadia is in the service lane on the compound's eastern face. She arrived twenty-two minutes ago and has been moving at the pace of a maintenance contractor — purposeful, unremarkable. The ribs tell her the pace is the correct pace. She does not argue with them.

She has Morrow's maintenance logs. Not from Morrow directly; from the documentation set that routes through the ORACLE PRIME infrastructure access layer, which she extracted three days ago and has been reading in sections ever since. The HALO UNIT service access points are documented for routine maintenance. The documentation assumes anyone reading it is a maintenance contractor who needs to know where the access panels are.

She is a maintenance contractor who needs to know where the access panels are.

The panel is at shoulder height on the eastern service wall — a brushed-metal rectangle the size of a large book, no label, the kind of panel that a person without context would not look at twice. She already knows that it does not require a biometric read. It requires a six-digit maintenance code, which rotates on a fourteen-day cycle, and which she obtained from the same documentation set.

She looks left. The patrol interval for the eastern service lane runs every eleven minutes. She arrived in minute three. She has been using minute four through eight to approach. She is in minute eight.

She enters the code.

The panel opens. Not a door — a relay access point, an interior passage the width of her shoulders that runs behind the building's outer skin and connects to the basement service corridor. It is not comfortable. It is not designed to be comfortable. It is designed for the cable plant and the maintenance techs who service it, and it accommodates them at the cost of everything else.

She moves through.

Her left hand braces against the passage wall — the right side is the side she cannot lean on; the ribs have opinions about pressure from the right. She moves at the correct pace. In the darkness of the passage, the only sound is the building's infrastructure — the ventilation, the cable conduit vibration, the low electrical hum of the security system running its continuous cycle — and, from somewhere ahead, a voice.

THE HERALD (V.O.)
(distant, through the building's internal speaker system — slightly wrong register)
It appears that our subject is currently moving through the secondary transit district, approximately four kilometers from the compound perimeter —

She stops.

She knows that voice. She has been listening to it for weeks. She knows the difference between its normal register and what she is hearing now — the 'It appears that' where it would say 'Confirmed:', the four-kilometer distance that is not her distance.

The decoy is still running.

She checks her internal count. She calculates she has between sixteen and twenty minutes before the base layer's degraded spatial data corrects far enough that the false position signal becomes unreliable. The Herald will not announce the correction; it will simply begin reporting a different location, and anyone paying close attention to the betting interface will notice the shift.

Sixteen minutes. Maybe fewer.

She moves.

The service passage opens into the basement corridor — a long, institutional space, fluorescent, the kind of corridor that exists in every large building and is never thought about by anyone who doesn't have to maintain it. Hilda Krast's architecture has cameras in the basement corridor. She knows their positions and their coverage angles. She knows that there is a section of the corridor, eleven meters long, that falls outside all coverage — a maintenance gap documented in the security audit report from four years ago, which was filed and has not been remediated.

Hilda Krast does not know Nadia has the security audit report from four years ago.

She reaches the coverage gap. Pauses. Breathes. The shallow breath of a person managing cracked ribs — controlled, deliberate, the kind of breathing that becomes automatic after enough practice. She has had enough practice.

She is inside VELTAIN.

She knows what this means. She knows that everything outside this building — the street grid, the escape routes, the theoretical possibility of retreat — is now on the other side of the architecture she just moved through. She is not outside anymore. She is in Roman's world, in its most controlled and most vulnerable configuration, and the clock that matters is the one she is already running.

Sixteen minutes. Maybe fewer.

She moves toward the interior stairs.
