INT. NEWSROOM — SERA'S DESK — NIGHT

The room is past deadline. The overheads are on economy setting — two-thirds of the floor dark. SERA has pulled her chair to the secondary screen, the one not visible from the main editorial floor, and she is running a cross-reference she started three days ago and keeps not finishing.

She has been working the Rennmark numbers for five months. Not as a story — not officially. As a thread she cannot let go of. The financial architecture: corporate entities registered in three jurisdictions, a courier network whose transaction volumes don't match the declared cargo categories, and a supply chain structure that keeps appearing adjacent to events she has reported on for unrelated reasons.

The cross-reference is comparing transaction IDs from a secondary logistics database — not the main registry, a supplemental system that was briefly public during a regulatory audit two years ago and has since gone dark — against a list of entity names she has been building.

The query runs.

She gets a result she did not expect to get tonight.

KELLART, MARCUS. Logistics controller, Rennmark supply chain. Registration address that cross-references to a shell entity — KELLART TRANSIT MANAGEMENT, registered in a jurisdiction she has seen before in this investigation. Inactive. No declared employees. Transaction patterns that don't read as logistics: irregular intervals, no seasonal variation, no volume correlation with shipping data.

She stares at the screen.

This is not a real business. She has seen enough real logistics companies to know the difference. This is a name attached to an operational function — someone who exists in the paper architecture of Roman's network in a specific role, and whose paper identity is thin enough to tear.

She does not know who Kellart is. She has a name and a structure.

She makes a decision before she has consciously finished making it: she opens her personal laptop, the one she does not connect to the newsroom network, and starts a new document.

She begins typing what she knows. Date. Query source. Result. What she can document versus what she is inferring. She is moving fast — she types the way she thinks, in clusters, half-formed sentences that she will clean up later, running commentary on what she is looking at in real time.

She does not run this through the editorial system. She does not open a story file. She is building a parallel record.

A sound from the corridor — MORVAN passing through with his coat on, heading for the door, sees her desk lamp still on.

MORVAN
(stopping at the end of her row)
You're still here.

SERA
Just finishing something.

MORVAN
The Rennmark piece.

A beat. Not a question.

SERA
Background. Nothing current.

MORVAN
Sera.

He comes a few steps closer. He has a specific tone he uses when he is about to convert an editorial disagreement into a liability discussion — slightly lower, slightly slower than his normal register.

MORVAN (CONT'D)
In terms of our exposure on this one — we've talked about this. The documentation chain doesn't support what you're trying to build, and the sources you've identified are all either unconfirmable or compromised by the regulatory context. Right now it's a research file. I need it to stay a research file.

SERA
I know.

MORVAN
I'm not asking you to drop it permanently. I'm asking you to hold it until you have something documentable. You don't have something documentable.

SERA
I know. I'm just running a background check. It's not — it's not a story build, it's indexing. For later.

He looks at her for a moment. He is not entirely convinced. He has learned, over the time they have worked together, that when Sera says "just" she usually means "exactly, and I have already decided to continue." But he also knows that she is careful — careful past the point of his patience, sometimes — and he decides to take the apparent compliance.

MORVAN
Deadline tomorrow is the Halverson filing, not this. I need you fresh.

SERA
I'll be out of here in twenty minutes.

He nods. He leaves.

She watches the corridor until she can no longer hear his footsteps.

Then she turns back to her personal laptop and keeps typing.

She is not thinking about Morvan. She is thinking about Kellart — the name against the structure, the structure against the pattern, the pattern against five months of trying to make something that keeps almost cohering actually cohere.

She has a subject. She doesn't know who he is. He is probably not the center of whatever she is building toward — too thin, too low in the architecture to be anything other than a functional node. But he is human. The investigation has a face, even if she can't see it yet.

She annotates the entry: KELLART. POSSIBLE SUBJECT — UNCONFIRMED. PARALLEL RECORD ONLY.

She closes the cross-reference tab, clears the query history, and logs out of the supplemental database.

She keeps typing on her personal machine.

She does not know that the query she ran — the cross-reference against the supplemental logistics database — generated an automated flag in a monitoring layer she has no way to know exists. The flag reads: external query, supply chain adjacency, unaffiliated source, journalist-pattern access. It is logged. A name will eventually be attached to it.

She packs up twenty minutes later, as promised.

The document on her personal laptop has forty-two lines. She saves it to local storage only. Closes the machine. Picks up her bag.

She is thinking about how to find a second source for Kellart.

She doesn't know that something has already started looking for her.
