INT. KASPAR TERMINAL — STAFF ROOM — EVENING

The light through the window has changed. Several hours.

She is on the floor. She got down there deliberately when her legs stopped being reliable — that clinical decision, the same one she would make for anyone: minimize fall distance, back to the wall, legs extended, coat as a layer between her and the cold floor. Her bag is beside her. The door is locked.

Her temperature has been climbing since the Halo Unit left. She knows the number without a thermometer: the specific combination of heat at the skin surface and cold in the core and the pressure behind the eyes that she has assessed in field patients enough times to have an internal calibration. Thirty-nine, probably. Moving toward thirty-nine-five.

Her thinking is slipping at the edges. Not gone. Permeable.

Marcus is in the room.

He is not there. She is aware that he is not there. The fever has made the membrane between what she knows and what she involuntarily assembles thinner, and what she has been carrying about him for eleven days has come through.

He is standing near the window — not at it. A foot back. The posture she knows. He is looking at her.

She does not find this comforting. The version of him the fever has produced is accurate: it includes everything.

MARCUS
(her memory; her processing)
You knew the structure of what he was.

NADIA
I was inside it.

MARCUS
You managed inside it. That's not the same thing.

She knows that. She has always known that. Eleven days of event and she has not looked at it directly because looking at it directly would have been the kind of thing that incapacitated her. The fever has removed that option.

She was inside Roman's system for four years. She performed the marriage, the household, the role — with enough precision that Roman never found a reliable reason to act. Her performance was also a structure he operated around. She chose, repeatedly, to stay, because leaving was too costly and because she believed staying protected Jin.

NADIA
I was protecting Jin.

MARCUS
Yes.

He does not argue with this. The version of him the fever produces is not cruel. It is accurate, which is not the same as kind.

MARCUS (CONT'D)
It's also true that you stayed because leaving required a
plan you didn't have yet.

Both things. She knows both things.

She is angry at him.

NADIA
You planned for your own death.

MARCUS
I planned for possibilities.

NADIA
You knew. You were already building the package when you sat
across from me in that room. You had the scrap of paper
ready. You had Otto ready. You had the contact chain already
running. You built a system for being gone and you sat
across from me in that room and you said you liked
redundancies and you didn't tell me that one of the
redundancies was you being dead.

He touches the back of his neck. That specific gesture. The fever has reproduced it exactly.

MARCUS
Would you have let me.

She does not answer.

He would not have been able to tell her the full scope of what he was doing without her trying to modify the plan. She would have tried to reduce his exposure. He knew this. He built the plan around knowing this.

NADIA
You left me the tools and then you left.

MARCUS
Yes.

NADIA
That's not—

She doesn't finish. There isn't a word for what it is. The word for what it is would require a register she does not have.

He sits down. The chair she was sitting in earlier. He has the posture she knows: hands loose on the table, nothing performed. He is looking at the table surface the way he looked at it in the rented room when he was thinking about what to say next.

MARCUS
Karev.

She looks at him. The fever's reproduction of him is very specific. The back of the scar on his index finger. The quality of his stillness.

MARCUS (CONT'D)
I like redundancies.

She heard him say this in the rented room. She filed it as operational caution. She did not understand it then. The fever is not giving her new information. It is giving her the same information with the suppression removed.

He planned for his own death because the alternative was planning for her death and Jin's, and the redundancy he built was a system that would work without him in it, which means the system was built for her to use, which means she is not a person carrying a dead man's tools.

She is the deployment. He built it for her to be the deployment.

She sits with this for a long time. The fever has not broken. It is moving through its architecture the way fevers do — she knows the stages; she knows what comes next; she knows that thirty-nine-five is not thirty-nine-nine; she knows that she has time.

The version of him in the staff room is fading. Not gone all at once. The fever does not do things all at once. The window has gone dark. She is still on the floor.

She is still alive.

She pulls the coat tighter. She closes her eyes.

She is still angry at him. That is fine. The anger is fuel the same as the rest of it.

She doesn't need to have resolved it to use it.

The room is quiet. Outside, the terminal continues to operate. The dock sounds have shifted from freight to overnight maintenance — a different rhythm, the specific sound of a facility in its low-activity cycle.

She is on the floor of a staff room in a terminal she entered through a propped door, eleven days into an event that is trying to kill her, with a wound infection and a compromised knee and no confirmed allies and a dead man's tools in her head.

She has been here before. Not this floor. This here.

She knows what comes next.

She waits for the fever to pass.
