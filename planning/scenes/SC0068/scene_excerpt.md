INT. PUBLIC FACILITY — KASPAR DISTRICT — LATE MORNING

A transit washroom in the port district's northern edge. Tiled. A mirror above the sink. A door with a slide lock. Cold running water. No camera she can find in the three seconds she gives to looking.

She slides the lock.

She puts her bag on the floor and opens the jacket and removes the dressing.

She has been doing this by feel for twelve hours. In the mirror, she can see it for the first time since the safe house.

She looks for a moment. Then she begins.

She presses her fingertips to her wrist pulse point. Elevated. She gives it a number: one hundred and four. She files the number.

The erythema is visible — a ring around the wound margins, extending approximately two centimeters beyond the suture line on the superior aspect and three on the inferior. The color is the specific red of subcutaneous infection, not the pink of normal tissue response. She has seen this color before. She knows what it means.

She palpates the wound margins in order: proximal, distal, superior, inferior.

The warmth is diffuse. Not the focal heat of tissue deciding — this is the settled warmth of tissue that has already decided. The geography of the heat covers an area she can bracket with both hands and still not contain.

She checks the suture line for integrity. The sutures are intact. Eight interrupted, each placed with the same measured tension. Tobias's hand is still in them — the consistency of spacing, the knotting method, the way none of them have torn through. He did this correctly. She notes this separately from what the wound is telling her, because it is a different piece of information.

At the inferior margin of the suture line, there is early seropurulent exudate. A small quantity. The beginning of what comes next.

She runs the assessment once more to confirm it.

The confirmation is the same.

She uses what she has: the remnants of the dressing she has been carrying, folded to the clean side. She applies compression at the wound margins in the specific pattern for managing soft tissue infection without irrigation — pressure and then release, ten seconds each cycle, four cycles, reading the tissue response with her fingertips. There is no irrigation solution. There is cold tap water, which is not sterile and which she uses anyway, careful to keep it away from the suture line.

She does this for approximately six minutes.

It is the correct technique for the available resources. She is doing it correctly.

It is not enough.

She knows this before she finishes. She finishes anyway, because finishing is also correct.

She looks at the wound in the mirror one more time before redressing it.

The assessment is not complicated. She has the vocabulary for this precisely because her background gives it to her. Without systemic antibiotics — not topical, not improvised, systemic — the infection will progress to the tissue plane within three to five days. Tissue plane infection produces fever. Fever produces cognitive impairment. Cognitive impairment in her current situation produces a specific outcome that she does not need to name.

She needs a doctor.

She needs one who will treat her without reporting her, who will have antibiotics, who will not ask questions she cannot answer, who will not see her face and connect it to the event that has been running for eleven days at peak broadcast visibility.

She does not have one.

She replaces the dressing. She zips the jacket. She picks up the bag.

She looks at the mirror for a moment.

She slides the lock and leaves.
