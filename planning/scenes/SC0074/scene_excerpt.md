INT. KASPAR TERMINAL — STAFF ROOM — NIGHT

Chen Mei is gone. The staff room is empty. The break table is empty. The polymer sleeve is gone with her.

Nadia is sitting on the bench with her back against the wall.

She presses her fingertips to her wrist pulse point. She counts. Ninety-two beats per minute. Elevated. Not dangerous. The fever has moved — thirty-seven point six. She notes it. Does not react.

She has been in this building for nineteen hours. The wound in her side — a friction injury from the transport cage, compressed through three performance blocks — has stopped bleeding and started stiffening. The muscle group is compensating. She will lose two to three degrees of lateral rotation on her left side before morning. She catalogs this the way she catalogs everything: as a constraint, not a narrative.

She is alive. She is functional. These are facts.

There is an exit.

She has always known about the exit. Roman's game has a withdrawal protocol — he stated it publicly, in the Herald's terms structure, four times across the game's public record. A contestant may formally withdraw at any broadcast checkpoint. Roman defines the resolution terms. Withdrawal is available. It is not a trap. It is a feature. He designed it to make the cage feel like a choice.

She knows this because Roman told her the shape of the lock before the game began, in a room where she understood that knowing the shape was not the same as having the key.

If she withdraws — formally, at the next checkpoint — Roman gets his demonstration. The game ends with the version of the story where the system won. He lets her go. She has studied him long enough to believe this. He is too precise to break his own protocol when the protocol is doing the work for him.

Jin comes later. Not immediately. Roman would keep Jin as a managed cost — proof that the terms are his, for as long as he needs proof. But eventually. He would not need the leverage forever.

She does the calculation.

She does it precisely, with no shortcuts, the way she would assess a patient before committing to a treatment protocol. She does not allow herself to round numbers in her favor.

She sits with it.

The terminal hums around her. Somewhere in the broadcast grid, the Herald is cycling through its update interval. The game is proceeding without her active participation. She can feel the architecture of it — nineteen hours of learned rhythms, monitoring intervals, the dead spots in the camera rotation.

She knows the time of the next broadcast checkpoint. She knows where the formal withdrawal initiates.

She sits with the calculation.

She stops sitting with it.

Not because the numbers changed. The numbers are the same. The exit is real; the protocol is real; the word "eventually" is real.

She knows what "eventually" means inside a system built on leverage. She has been inside enough of them. The military inquiry that cleared everyone. The years in Roman's household performing the appropriate version of herself until she stopped noticing she was performing. Every system where she chose the cage because the exposed field had already cost her once.

Eventually is how leverage works. You surrender the proof of your compliance, and they hold the proof, and the person you needed them to release becomes the permanent evidence of what you agreed to. You have made them a fixture.

She knows this. She has known it longer than she has known anything in this building.

She thinks of Jin.

Not abstractly. Not "my son" as a category. Specifically.

He weighs eleven point four kilograms. She knows this because two weeks before the game began she lifted him and her hands registered the weight with the precision she cannot turn off. Eleven point four. He had been sick the week before; slightly underweight; she had noted it and scheduled a follow-up appointment she is not going to take him to now.

His resting breathing rate is twenty-two breaths per minute. She knows this because she counted it watching him sleep, three nights before the game took him, standing in the doorway for six minutes because she could not make herself leave the doorway. Twenty-two breaths. Even. No laboring.

These are not memories. They are data points. She uses them as data points because data points do not make her nonfunctional and memories would.

Eleven point four kilograms. Twenty-two breaths per minute. Roman's leverage has a resting breathing rate.

She stands up.

The wound in her side pulls with the movement. She accepts the rotation loss on her left without adjusting her expression. She straightens to parade-ground uprightness — the last time she will use this posture for its original purpose, though she does not know that yet.

The exit is real. The calculation is complete. She knows it entirely and she is not taking it.

Not because she believes she will win. She does not know if she will win. She knows only that "eventually" is a cage she has agreed to live in before and that she will not agree to it again.

The next broadcast checkpoint opens in thirty-eight minutes.

She has work to do.
