INT./EXT. CACHE SITE — OUTER TRANSIT HUB — NIGHT

The transit hub has been decommissioned. Lights still working on one wing. The rest runs on emergency battery — orange strip lighting at floor level, no overhead. A place people pass through when they want not to be seen.

Zara knows this cache. She has known it for four months. She did not choose to remember it. She remembered it anyway, the way professionals remember things — not because it was important and not because it wasn't.

They approach from the service corridor. No words. Zara takes the left. Nadia reads it and takes the right.

The first operative is at the corner. He doesn't hear them coming. Nadia is faster than Zara expected. It is not clean. It is fast.

The second is inside the access corridor. Zara takes him before he finishes turning. Three seconds. He goes down. He is breathing. That is the most they can offer him.

The third is at the cache door itself, stationed with professional patience — earpiece in, weight balanced, not reading his phone. He is better than the first two.

He is not better than both of them.

Zara takes the entry point. Nadia goes through.

---

The cache is a converted maintenance bay. Metal shelving. Plastic totes numbered and stacked with the obsessive order of people who live by what they can reach in the dark.

Zara goes directly to the far wall. She has been here before. She knows where the good totes are.

Nadia works the near shelves. She does not rush. She reads labels. She takes what the list requires and nothing that isn't on it.

Medical: field dressings, hemostatic agent, two pressure applicators, topical antibiotic — the good kind, not the black-market grade that does nothing — one IV kit, sealed. She takes two of the antibiotic.

She adds one morphine syrette. She puts it in a different pocket from the rest.

Comms: two short-range encrypted units, charged. One longer-range transmitter — older model, but it works on a frequency Roman's network doesn't monitor in this district. She takes all three.

Zara brings weapons to the center of the room. Lays them out. Two compact sidearms, one suppressed. One blade, fixed, military specification. Spare magazines, a full box of case-rounded ammunition.

---

Nadia reaches for the top shelf. Her right arm pulls. Something in the shoulder that has been quiet for two days says, without warning, that it is not as quiet as she thought.

She takes what she came for. Her arm completes the reach. The shelf doesn't know anything happened.

She moves to the weapons and begins to load.

Zara, without looking: her assessment is in her stillness. She does not offer to help. She does not draw attention to what she saw. She resumes packing.

---

It is Zara who finds the documents.

A flat portfolio, plastic-sheathed, wedged between two totes on the second-to-bottom shelf. Not labeled. Not locked. The kind of thing you only hide if you know what you're looking for.

She opens it. Scans the first page. Then the second.

She says nothing for four seconds.

Then she takes it to the nearest clear surface — the top of a packing case — and spreads it open.

---

A deployment map. Hand-annotated. Timestamps current: the most recent notation is from eleven hours ago.

Roman's hunter assets mapped across the approach grid. Seven active teams. Coverage corridors marked in two colors — primary and secondary. Dense along the eastern approach. Dense through the mid-district. Dense everywhere except:

One corridor. Running northwest, through the abandoned port infrastructure, skirting the outer perimeter of a decommissioned industrial zone, arriving at VELTAIN's service boundary at a point that has one notated guard cycle: two guards, ninety-minute rotation, no camera coverage at the junction.

Zara's finger rests on the gap.

She does not say anything.

Nadia has moved to her left shoulder. She is already looking.
