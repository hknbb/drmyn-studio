INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — EAST EQUIPMENT ROOM — CONTINUOUS

The east equipment room is a climate-control and power distribution node — ceiling-mounted hardware, floor conduit, two access panels. It has a security checkpoint at the entry door: a manned station, normally. Currently: empty. The officer's terminal is still active; the coup alert is scrolling across it. She reads it in passing without slowing: SECOND ACTIVE BREACH — ALL AVAILABLE PERSONNEL. The personnel responded. The checkpoint is the gap.

Zone 1: clear. Seven seconds. She is through.

The next door — interior access to the main building's lower level — is across a short connecting passage. Twenty meters. One more security station. This one is manned.

She stops at the equipment room's far wall and looks.

The officer at station is forty meters away, in the connecting passage, facing the interior access door. They have a sidearm, a communications piece in their ear, and a handheld terminal that is displaying the same coup alert. Their weight is shifted slightly to the left. Their shoulder tension reads as high — they have been receiving conflicting instructions for the last several minutes and have not yet been told which one takes priority.

Nadia reads this in the time it takes the officer to glance at their terminal.

She waits.

The officer's head turns toward the terminal. She moves.

Seven seconds. Measured, controlled, on the left side of the connecting passage where the overhead conduit provides a shadow line. The officer's head is still down when she reaches the far wall of the passage. She flattens against it. The interior access door is three meters to her right.

The officer looks up. The connecting passage is empty in the direction they check.

Zone 2: through.

INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — INTERIOR ACCESS PASSAGE — CONTINUOUS

She sits down.

Not a decision — a body decision that arrived before a conscious one. She is against the wall, weight off, head forward, eyes open and fixed on the floor. The specific quality of the floor: grey composite tile, slightly scuffed near the door frame. She looks at it with her full attention.

Vasovagal. Not cardiac. The distinction matters and she knows it immediately. Her heart rate had spiked in the Zone 2 crossing and the blood pressure drop that follows a sustained sympathetic peak has arrived on schedule. Her field medicine training names it: a pre-syncopal event, manageable, not a crisis. The intervention is simple: positional correction, controlled breathing, four seconds. After four seconds the peripheral vascular resistance will partially restore and she will be able to stand without a position-change collapse.

She breathes. One cycle. Her eyes stay open.

She counts the seconds that she cannot count toward the override window.

Four.

She gets up. Nothing in her movement shows the four seconds just happened.

The interior access door has a credential lock — a separate system from the service corridor's maintenance channel. The same device she used at the HALO UNIT has a secondary command sequence for access credential systems on the VELTAIN compound network; she extracted this from the same maintenance documentation where she found the override command, six days ago, from eleven meters, in poor light, from Morrow's hands.

She enters the sequence. The device sends. The lock considers it for twelve seconds — the longest twelve seconds she has experienced since she watched Tobias start the suture in MERIN — and then the indicator light shifts from red to amber.

Not green. Amber.

Amber means: partial authorization. The door will open. It will log the access under a maintenance exception code, which will eventually flag for review. Not tonight. Tonight, amber is enough.

The door opens.

INT. VELTAIN COMPOUND — BUILDING INTERIOR — CONTINUOUS

She steps through. The door closes behind her with the specific sound of a well-machined seal engaging — solid, definitive, the acoustic signature of a threshold crossed.

The noise of the compound's exterior is muffled. The security response still ongoing outside is on the other side of two sealed doors. In here: the ambient sound of a climate-controlled private building at night — a hum, the low register of ventilation, the absence of any other person in immediate range.

She is inside.

Seven minutes, nineteen seconds.

The override window has forty-one seconds remaining. She does not need them.

She checks the closure tape on her left side. Two of the four strips are peeling at the edge. She presses them back with her thumb, firmly, without looking down. Her eyes are already reading the corridor ahead.

Interior focus.
