EXT./INT. THE REPAIR UNIT — EXTERNAL OBSERVATION POSITION — MORNING

She is behind the secondary ventilation stack on the east side of the unit — cover from the road-facing sensor corridors, structural shadow, partial sightline into the rear work area through an unshuttered internal window. She came in from the east during the ducting crew's active interval, using them as audio cover for the last thirty meters. The approach geometry she mapped in the early morning held.

She has been here eleven minutes.

---

The man inside is TOBIAS ORELL. She does not know his name yet.

What she can observe: the rear work area of the unit, approximately twelve by eight meters. A workbench along the north wall has been modified — elevated, fitted with a task light, surface covered in a material that is not for metal repair. The instruments on the surface are not repair instruments. They are medical instruments, or close enough that the distinction requires her medical training to identify, which is exactly the kind of distinction that separates a genuine operation from a staged one.

He is treating a forearm laceration on a man she cannot identify — a visitor from the district economy, based on the entry pattern she observed: walked in, not announced, not expected, familiar with the space. Not a patient in any scheduled sense. Someone who knocked.

She watches him work.

---

Assessment criteria, in order.

Behavioral consistency: does his activity pattern match the observed profile from the first reconnaissance, or does it show signs of staged waiting?

He was already mid-task when she arrived — the visitor had been there before her. She observed no interval of deliberate positioning, no environmental preparation that would suggest he arranged the space for observation. The workbench was already configured. The task light was already on. He was already doing the thing.

Technical authenticity: does the work she can observe read as trained practice?

The wound prep sequence is correct — she can read that from this distance. The suture is taking longer than it needs to, which means he is being careful rather than fast, which is what a practitioner with a real interest in the outcome does when they are not observed. He thinks he is not observed.

Environmental integrity: does the space show the accumulated grammar of genuine long-term use?

The instrument layout on the modified bench is a personal system. Suture scissors at an angle that is wrong for standard sterile field protocol but specific — not randomly misplaced, specifically positioned. Forceps to the left rather than the right, the handle orientation inverted. The kind of adaptation that accumulates when a person has done this alone, without a scrub nurse, often enough to develop muscle memory that overrides training. A plant briefed to stage a medical environment would orient closer to standard. This is not standard. This is someone's.

---

Through the window: the visitor says something she cannot hear.

Tobias pauses.

She watches the pause. It is approximately two seconds. She does not know what it means: either a practitioner completing a clinical thought before answering, or someone deciding how to perform the next beat of a role. She holds it in the unresolvable column and continues.

He answers. Whatever he says makes the visitor's posture change — not alarmed, a small recalibration. He nods. Tobias returns to the suture.

The pause was probably clinical. She cannot confirm this.

She does not need to.

---

What rules out the plant assessment, definitively:

The suture tension.

She has been watching him close the wound for four minutes. The tension on each bite is consistent — tight enough to approximate, not tight enough to compromise perfusion. That calibration is not in a textbook. It is in the hands of someone who has closed wounds under real conditions and learned from the ones that healed wrong and the ones that didn't heal at all.

You cannot fake suture tension calibration. You can fake the motions. You cannot fake the result. And you cannot fake the fact that the motion has become unconscious — he is not concentrating on the tension. He is concentrating on the wound.

That is the tell that rules out the plant.

She has been here eleven minutes. She has seen enough.

---

The dressing on her left forearm has approximately eighty minutes of integrity remaining. After that, the wound edge condition will begin to deteriorate — not catastrophically, but in ways that compound. She has been managing this across the last three hours with increasing attention. The window is not closed but it is narrowing.

She considers what she is about to do.

The first human contact she has chosen, since launch, is with a man she has been watching through a window for eleven minutes. She routes the observation through the assessment: his competence is the relevant variable. Everything else is noise.

She steps out from behind the ventilation stack.

She is now in the coverage zone. The HALO UNIT network has her. The broadcast has this location. Whatever happens in this unit, The Herald will know she came here.

She moves to the rear entrance and knocks twice, evenly.
