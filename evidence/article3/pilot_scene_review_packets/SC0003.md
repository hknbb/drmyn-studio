## Excerpt

```fountain
INT. VALE RESIDENCE — JIN'S ROOM — VARDOVA — MID-MORNING

The room is warm. Curtains drawn against the morning light, a white-noise machine running its low, steady note. A crib, a changing table, a small shelf of things that are his — soft shapes, a worn cloth bear, a plastic ring he chews on.

NADIA sits on the floor with her back against the crib, knees pulled up. JIN is in her lap, upright, eight months old, regarding her face with the full serious attention of someone who has not yet learned to pretend.

She is not performing anything. There is no one to perform for.

NADIA
There you are.

Jin makes a sound — not language, not quite. An answer anyway.

She adjusts the thin hospital bracelet on his wrist — two fingers, a practiced half-turn to center the clasp — then leaves her hand loosely over his. The bracelet is white plastic with a small printed ID strip. His name. Her name listed as registrant.

He reaches up. Both hands. Finds her cheek.

She lets him.

For a moment her posture changes. The careful uprightness goes. Her shoulders drop. She tilts her face slightly into his hands the way a person tilts toward warmth — not a performance of maternal feeling but the thing itself, the involuntary version.

His fingers are very small and very warm and he is pressing on her cheekbone with the flat of his palm as if testing whether it is real.

NADIA (CONT'D)
Okay.

She says it quietly. Not to reassure him. To herself.

Then her eyes move.

Window: northwest-facing, curtains closed, no gap. Door: one point of entry, behind her — she notes the angle without turning. The shelf: unchanged since yesterday. The monitor on the changing table: light steady, green.

Her shoulders settle back to where they were.

Jin has not noticed. He is still studying her face. He finds her nose and examines it with the same focused precision she uses on everything.

She almost smiles.

NADIA (CONT'D)
He's going to want feeding in twenty minutes.

She says it to no one. Making a record. She does this — speaks the facts of his needs aloud in an empty room, as if naming them is a form of protection.

She looks at him for another moment. His ordinary weight in her lap. His ordinary warmth.

Then she lifts him, settles him against her shoulder, and stands.

The white-noise machine runs on.
```
