## Title and scope note

This document defines the production-facing visual rules for `CLOSING PRICE` using screenplay-grounded evidence only. It is downstream of `source/story_blueprint.md`, `source/character_dossier.md`, and the canonical screenplay. It is not a prompt pack, not a mood board, and not an Omni schema.

## Visual thesis

`CLOSING PRICE` should look like a controlled world that keeps revealing the machinery under its own finish. The image system is built from surveillance pressure, institutional design, physical depletion, and small domestic details that become operational facts.

Confirmed recurring tendencies:
- Wealth reads as control, curation, and underuse, not warmth.
- Threat is often legible through geometry before it is legible through action.
- Broadcast-space imagery is cleaner and more finished than lived-space imagery.
- Physical cost must remain visible in posture, gait, skin, wound management, and pace.

Conservative likely tendencies:
- The closer Nadia gets to Roman's core infrastructure, the more the image shifts from domestic restraint to hard institutional precision.
- The film's strongest visual contrasts come from lived-body texture versus machine/interface cleanliness.

## Core cinematic register

- Grounded near-future, not speculative spectacle.
- Physical and procedural, not ornamental.
- Legible spatial geography over impressionistic fragmentation.
- Surveillance-aware without becoming full surveillance POV grammar.
- Emotional intensity delivered through bodily detail and environmental fact, not visual excess.

## Global tone-to-image rules

- If the story beat is intimate, stay with material detail, breathing, posture, and object handling rather than adding soft romanticism.
- If the story beat is tactical, the image must privilege exits, sightlines, exposure, cover, and route logic.
- If the story beat is broadcast-facing, the image may become cleaner and more typographic, but it must still feel like a profit machine, not a game-show.
- If the story beat contains injury or fatigue, the frame must preserve consequence rather than hiding deterioration.
- Violence must feel expensive in time and recovery, not merely intense in the instant.

## Palette rules

Confirmed recurring palette evidence:
- Pale stone, muted cream, and cold domestic neutrals in the Vale residence.
- Grey water, concrete, diesel, and salt-washed industrial tones in MERIN.
- Grey-orange industrial haze and corrugated infrastructure in ZORAL CORRIDOR spaces.
- Functional whites, steel, concrete, and muted utility colors in KASPAR TERMINAL and VELTAIN service zones.
- High-contrast interface whites/ambers against controlled dark fields in ORACLE PRIME layers.
- Small precise accent colors matter when source-grounded: the pale blue/white infant bracelet, green monitor light, rust-orange standpipe staining.

Operational rule:
- Saturation should come from actual environment or interface logic, not decorative grading.
- Luxury spaces should not drift into rich jewel-tone opulence unless source text explicitly supports it.

Do not do:
- Neon cyberpunk color design.
- Teal-orange blockbuster simplification.
- Golden sentimental domestic warmth as a default treatment.

## Exposure / contrast rules

- Interiors should remain readable at low exposure levels; avoid crushed blacks that hide practical geography.
- Night scenes should preserve object edges, doorways, and route information.
- Daylight in this project is often cold, diffused, overcast, or partially blocked rather than radiant.
- Broadcast layers may run cleaner contrast than lived spaces, but still should not bloom into showmanship.
- Highlight control matters in polished elite environments; matte or dirty roll-off is more appropriate in industrial and transit environments.

## Texture / image-surface rules

- Prioritize surfaces that show use, control, neglect, or curation: pale stone, concrete, glass, steel, cheap plastic, brushed institutional metal, folded linens, paper napkins, worn cloth, damp concrete, dust-shadow, shelf glass.
- Smoothness should read as expensive management, not visual softness.
- Dirt, sweat, rust, dampness, adhesive residue, wound dressings, and scuffed work surfaces should accumulate across the film.
- Interfaces and machine surfaces should feel engineered and maintained, not fantastical.

## Camera behavior rules

- Stay aligned with Nadia's operational reading whenever possible.
- Camera choices should help the audience read what she reads: a frame angle, a camera sightline, a blind spot, a route, a gate, a lock, a hand movement.
- Use observational discipline before using expressive flourish.
- When coverage widens, it should clarify geometry or institutional scale, not merely add grandeur.
- The film may briefly adopt system-facing observation in broadcast contexts, but lived scenes should not lose human physicality.

## Framing rules

- Thresholds, corridors, windows, doors, gates, desks, crib rails, shelving, and checkpoint edges are recurring framing anchors.
- Frame bodies against systems of control: doorframes, sightlines, camera arcs, gate lanes, access readers, service passages.
- Hold on objects when they are operational evidence, not decorative inserts.
- Domestic frames should often reveal how little of the residence is genuinely lived in.
- Avoid over-coverage that destroys route clarity in tactical scenes.

## Movement rules

- Default movement is measured, exact, and motivated.
- Fast movement should emerge from tactical necessity, not from a general handheld restlessness.
- Pursuit scenes may accelerate, but spatial legibility must survive the acceleration.
- Broadcast movement should feel professionally controlled rather than ecstatic.
- When Nadia is physically degraded, camera movement should respect reduced capacity instead of pretending she is moving cleanly.

## Lighting rules

- Practical-source logic first.
- Elite domestic interiors: dim, controlled, often partially occluded daylight or low practical pools.
- Industrial/transit spaces: strip lighting, sodium spill, overcast daylight, service-light spill, monitor glow.
- Waterfront and quay spaces: grey reflected light, salt/diesel atmosphere, hard utility fixtures where needed.
- Broadcast space: polished, clean, high legibility, no carnival lighting.
- Light must support the story distinction between watched space and lived space.

## Interior rules

- Domestic interiors in Roman's world should feel curated, expensive, and emotionally withheld.
- Workrooms, studies, trophy rooms, and control spaces should reveal function through arrangement, not exposition.
- Safe houses and informal shelters should feel partially practical, partially provisional, never design-forward.
- Institutional interiors should retain enough specificity that a person could work there.

## Exterior rules

- Exterior spaces should feel weathered, infrastructural, and used.
- Roads, gates, loading zones, wall walks, quay edges, freight corridors, and approach lanes matter more than skyline spectacle.
- City life should remain plausible and ordinary around extraordinary events.
- Exterior night should preserve operational visibility: where cover is, where cameras are, where the next turn is.

## Day / dawn / dusk / night distinctions

- Dawn: cold, low-energy, exposed, transitional; strong for drop, approach, and post-night tactical recalibration.
- Day: flat or controlled rather than celebratory; institutional and industrial realism should dominate.
- Dusk: useful for threshold crossings, public-space ambiguity, and mixed civilian/broadcast pressure.
- Night: privacy never equals safety; night is often the cleanest arena for surveillance, tactical movement, and institutional calm.

## Human face / skin / fatigue rendering rules

- Faces must carry exhaustion, dehydration, pain, stress, and decision cost.
- Nadia's fatigue should progress visibly across the film; do not reset her to a clean baseline after action passages.
- Injuries should be readable in swelling, guarded movement, dressing state, and skin tone shift.
- Roman's face should not read as florid or operatic; the threat comes from control, composure, and tiny slips.
- Infant rendering must remain ordinary, specific, and unsentimental.

## Wardrobe rendering rules

- Wardrobe should communicate class system, job function, concealment, or wear burden without over-design.
- Nadia's clothing should increasingly read as survival-use clothing rather than composed domestic presentation.
- Workwear in terminal, dock, transit, and industrial spaces should look materially credible and seasonally lived-in.
- Elite wardrobe should stay disciplined and controlled, not fashion-editorial.

## Prop rendering rules

- Props are often evidence carriers: water glass, frame angle, folded linens, formula tin, bracelet, phone, access card, med kit, terminal kettle, cloned credential, shutdown card.
- If a prop matters, show why it matters materially.
- Baby objects should remain ordinary and specific, not stylized nursery dressing.
- Roman's objects should read as provenance and judgment, not generalized luxury clutter.
- Broadcast interfaces should look like financial instrumentation first, entertainment second.

## Violence / injury depiction rules

- Impact, cut, infection risk, suturing, gait change, and recovery delay are all part of the image language.
- Do not aestheticize blood spray or injury for spectacle alone.
- Medical handling should read procedurally exact when source supports it.
- Aftermath is part of the action grammar and must remain on screen in later scenes.

## Broadcast-world vs lived-world visual distinction rules

- Broadcast world: clean, high-contrast, typographic, calibrated, emotionally false but professionally persuasive.
- Lived world: irregular, tactile, weathered, bodily, and full of partial information.
- The interface should feel like a market tool.
- The human world should feel like a place people sleep, work, bleed, hide, and carry children through.
- Do not let broadcast imagery visually dominate the lived story beyond the moments where the machine is exerting pressure.

## Negative visual rules

- No glossy near-future luxury fetish.
- No comic-book violence.
- No beauty-light default on faces under stress.
- No abstract drone language that obscures tactical geography.
- No omniscient spectacle camera that knows more than the scene has earned.
- No sentimental mother-and-child glow that contradicts the film's physical realism.

## Do-not-drift list

- Do not drift from grounded near-future into overt science fiction.
- Do not drift from physical cost into action invulnerability.
- Do not drift from institutional realism into generic villain lairs.
- Do not drift from surveillance geometry into chaotic coverage.
- Do not drift from precise object meaning into decorative production design.
- Do not drift from cold profit-machine broadcast logic into camp media satire.
